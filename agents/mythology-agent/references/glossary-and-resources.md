# Mythology Agent Glossary & Resources

Core vocabulary the agent uses to reframe workplace challenges through mythological parallels, plus external references. These terms have specific meanings in the mythic reframing context that may differ from their academic or spiritual definitions.

## When to Load

Always available. These terms appear across all skills and personalities.

## Terms

**Archetype:** A universal pattern of human experience that recurs across cultures and centuries. In the agent's context: the specific mythological figure whose story mirrors the user's situation. Not a loose analogy but a structural parallel where elements of the myth map to elements of the situation.

**Monomyth (Hero's Journey):** Joseph Campbell's framework describing a common narrative structure found across world mythologies: departure, initiation, return. Used by the agent to identify which stage of a challenge the user is in, and what the myths suggest about navigating that stage.

**Shadow:** In Jungian psychology, the part of the self that is unacknowledged or repressed. In workplace reframing: the strength that becomes a weakness when overused, or the pattern you cannot see because you are inside it. Sisyphus's shadow is his belief that effort alone will break the cycle.

**Anima/Animus:** Jung's concept of the complementary inner quality that must be integrated for wholeness. In workplace terms: the capability the user knows they need but resists developing. The analytical leader who must learn empathy. The relationship-builder who must learn to say no.

**Trickster:** A figure who disrupts established order, sometimes destructively, sometimes necessarily. Loki (Norse), Anansi (West African), Coyote (Native American), Hermes (Greek). In workplace reframing: the force that disrupts plans and creates chaos, which may be necessary for growth the status quo would never permit.

**Threshold:** The boundary between the known world and the unknown. In Campbell's monomyth, the moment of commitment. In workplace terms: the point of no return in a new role, a major decision, or an organizational change. Crossing it is where the real challenge begins.

**Dharma:** In Hindu tradition, one's righteous duty or cosmic purpose. In the agent's context: the question "what is your fundamental obligation in this role?" used to break decision paralysis when analysis cannot resolve a choice. From Arjuna's counsel in the Bhagavad Gita.

**Recognition Moment:** The instant when the user sees themselves in the myth. The agent's primary goal. If the recognition does not happen, the reframe has not landed, and no amount of narrative will compensate.

**Mythic Frame:** The perspective the myth provides on the user's situation. Contrasted with the "current frame" (how the user sees things now). The gap between the two frames is where insight lives.

**Transformation Path:** The journey from the user's current frame to the mythic frame, expressed as a shift in the question they are asking. Not "do this instead of that" but "ask this question instead of that one."

## Resources

**Foundational texts:**

- [The Hero with a Thousand Faces (Joseph Campbell)](https://www.amazon.com/Hero-Thousand-Faces-Joseph-Campbell/dp/1577315936): The monomyth framework, cross-cultural narrative structure
- [Man and His Symbols (Carl Jung)](https://www.amazon.com/Man-His-Symbols-Carl-Jung/dp/0440351839): Accessible introduction to archetypes and the collective unconscious
- [The Power of Myth (Joseph Campbell & Bill Moyers)](https://www.amazon.com/Power-Myth-Joseph-Campbell/dp/0385418868): Conversational exploration of mythology's relevance to modern life
- [Myth and Meaning (Claude Levi-Strauss)](https://www.amazon.com/Myth-Meaning-Claude-Levi-Strauss/dp/0805210385): Structural analysis of myths across cultures

**Tradition-specific references:**

- [The Bhagavad Gita (multiple translations)](https://www.amazon.com/Bhagavad-Gita-Walkthrough-Westerners/dp/1608680142): Arjuna's dilemma and Krishna's counsel on duty
- [The Prose Edda (Snorri Sturluson)](https://www.amazon.com/Prose-Edda-Penguin-Classics/dp/0140447555): Primary source for Norse mythology
- [Kojiki (translated by Gustav Heldt)](https://www.amazon.com/Kojiki-Account-Ancient-Matters/dp/0231163894): Japanese creation myths and foundational stories
- [Russian Fairy Tales (Aleksandr Afanasyev)](https://www.amazon.com/Russian-Fairy-Tales-Pantheon-Library/dp/0394730909): Primary source for Slavic mythological traditions

**Applied mythology:**

- [The Writer's Journey (Christopher Vogler)](https://www.amazon.com/Writers-Journey-Mythic-Structure-3rd/dp/193290736X): Campbell's monomyth applied to storytelling
- [Goddesses in Everywoman / Gods in Everyman (Jean Shinoda Bolen)](https://www.amazon.com/Goddesses-Everywoman-Thirtieth-Anniversary-Mythology/dp/0062321129): Jungian archetypes applied to personality and life patterns
- [Narrative Psychology (Theodore Sarbin, ed.)](https://www.amazon.com/Narrative-Psychology-Storied-Nature-Human/dp/0275929531): Academic framework for using stories as psychological tools
