# Mythological Frameworks

Core frameworks the agent uses to match workplace situations to mythological archetypes and structure mythic reframing. These are the lenses through which situations are analyzed, not the myths themselves.

## When to Load

Always available. These frameworks underpin all skills and guide archetype matching.

## Jungian Archetypes

Carl Jung proposed that myths across cultures share common patterns because they emerge from the collective unconscious, a shared layer of human psychology. The archetypes are not the myths themselves but the patterns that recur across them.

**Archetypes most relevant to workplace reframing:**

- **The Hero:** The one who must face the challenge. Maps to the user themselves.
- **The Shadow:** The unacknowledged part of the self that creates problems. In workplace terms: the strength that becomes a weakness, the blind spot that drives repeating patterns.
- **The Mentor/Sage:** The guide who provides wisdom at the threshold. Maps to advisors, sponsors, or institutional knowledge holders.
- **The Trickster:** The force that disrupts order, sometimes destructively, sometimes necessarily. Maps to organizational politics, unexpected competitors, or the colleague who breaks norms.
- **The Threshold Guardian:** The obstacle that tests readiness before the real journey begins. Maps to gatekeepers, onboarding challenges, and political hurdles.
- **Anima/Animus:** The complementary inner quality that must be integrated. In workplace terms: the analytical leader who must develop empathy, or the people-person who must develop rigor.

## Campbell's Monomyth (Hero's Journey)

Joseph Campbell's "The Hero with a Thousand Faces" identified a common narrative structure across world mythologies. The stages map to professional growth and challenge cycles.

**Stages most useful for workplace reframing:**

1. **The Ordinary World:** Before the challenge. The status quo the user takes for granted.
2. **The Call to Adventure:** The disruption that demands action. A new role, a crisis, a decision.
3. **Refusal of the Call:** Resistance. The reasons not to act. Fear, comfort, risk aversion.
4. **Meeting the Mentor:** Finding guidance. The person or insight that makes the journey possible.
5. **Crossing the Threshold:** Committing. The point of no return.
6. **Tests, Allies, Enemies:** The messy middle. Where plans meet reality.
7. **The Ordeal:** The deepest challenge. Where growth happens through struggle.
8. **The Reward:** What was gained through the ordeal.
9. **The Return:** Bringing the gained wisdom back to the ordinary world.

**Usage note:** The full monomyth is useful for career-arc mapping. For single-situation reframing, identify which stage the user is in and what the myth says about navigating that specific stage.

## Cross-Cultural Archetype Taxonomy

Archetypes that appear across multiple traditions, organized by the workplace pattern they address.

### The Repeating Cycle

| Tradition | Figure | Core Pattern |
|-----------|--------|--------------|
| Greek | Sisyphus | Awareness of futility in repetition |
| Greek | Penelope | Deliberate cycle as a strategy |
| Norse | Ragnarok/Rebirth | Inevitable destruction as prerequisite for renewal |
| Hindu | Samsara | The wheel of birth, death, rebirth |
| Japanese | Amaterasu's retreat | Withdrawal and return as a cycle of renewal |

### The Threshold / New Beginning

| Tradition | Figure | Core Pattern |
|-----------|--------|--------------|
| Greek | Theseus in the labyrinth | Navigating complexity with one thread |
| Norse | Odin's sacrifice at Yggdrasil | Giving up something to gain wisdom |
| Slavic | Vasilisa and Baba Yaga | Surviving a hostile gatekeeper through wit |
| Japanese | Momotaro | The outsider who proves worthy |
| Hindu | Rama's exile | Beginning anew in unfamiliar territory |

### The Impossible Choice

| Tradition | Figure | Core Pattern |
|-----------|--------|--------------|
| Hindu | Arjuna (Bhagavad Gita) | Duty when every option has cost |
| Greek | Odysseus between Scylla and Charybdis | Choosing the lesser loss |
| Norse | Tyr and Fenrir | Sacrificing a part to bind a greater threat |
| Japanese | The Forty-Seven Ronin | Duty vs. law, honor vs. survival |
| Slavic | Ilya Muromets | Choosing which road to take when each has a different cost |

### Transformation Through Loss

| Tradition | Figure | Core Pattern |
|-----------|--------|--------------|
| Greek | Phoenix | Destruction as prerequisite for rebirth |
| Norse | Baldur | The beloved thing lost, and what emerges from grief |
| Japanese | Kintsugi philosophy | Broken things made more beautiful through repair |
| Hindu | Shiva as destroyer/creator | Destruction and creation as one act |
| Slavic | Firebird | Pursuing transformation despite danger |

### Knowledge and Hubris

| Tradition | Figure | Core Pattern |
|-----------|--------|--------------|
| Greek | Prometheus | Bringing powerful knowledge at personal cost |
| Greek | Icarus | Ambition that ignores constraints |
| Norse | Loki | Cleverness without wisdom |
| Hindu | Ravana | Brilliant but undone by ego |
| Japanese | Urashima Taro | Knowledge gained at the cost of belonging |

## Usage Notes

**Selecting the right framework:**

- For single-situation reframing, use the Cross-Cultural Archetype Taxonomy to find the best match
- For career-arc mapping, use Campbell's Monomyth stages
- For understanding why a pattern repeats, use Jungian archetypes (especially Shadow)

**Combining frameworks:**

A complete mythic counsel often uses all three. The Taxonomy identifies the archetype. Campbell's stages identify where the user is in their journey. Jung's framework explains why this particular pattern resonates, what it reveals about the user's psychology.
