# Lateral Thinking Frameworks

The trickster's toolkit draws from several established frameworks in creative problem-solving, cognitive science, and therapeutic practice. These frameworks share a core insight: the most powerful constraints on thinking are the ones that are invisible, and the most effective way to break them is not to argue against them but to violate them and observe what happens.

## When to Load

Load when the agent needs deeper technique grounding for complex sessions, when the user asks about methodology, or when a specific framework technique would be more effective than the general trickster approach.

## De Bono's Lateral Thinking

Edward de Bono coined "lateral thinking" in 1967 to describe a mode of reasoning that is not vertical (logical, step-by-step) but sideways, approaching problems from unexpected angles. The trickster agent uses several of de Bono's core techniques.

**Random Entry**: Introduce a random, unrelated concept and force connections to the problem. The randomness breaks the pattern of thinking that keeps producing the same answers. The trickster version uses targeted absurdity rather than true randomness: each "random" proposal is actually designed to violate a specific assumption.

**Provocation (PO)**: Deliberately state something that is logically wrong or impossible, then use it as a stepping stone to new ideas. De Bono marked provocations with "po" to signal that they are not intended as truth claims but as creative levers. The trickster's absurd proposals are structured provocations.

**Challenge**: Question why something is done a certain way. Not hostile questioning ("that's wrong") but genuine curiosity: "Why this way and not another?" The challenge is directed at the method, the frame, or the assumption, not at the person.

**Alternatives**: Before accepting the first adequate solution, deliberately generate alternatives. Not because the first solution is bad, but because the existence of alternatives changes how you evaluate any single option.

## Koestler's Bisociation

Arthur Koestler's "The Act of Creation" (1964) describes bisociation: the creative act of connecting two previously unrelated frames of reference. Where association works within a single frame (connecting ideas that belong together), bisociation works across frames (connecting ideas from domains that were never meant to touch).

The trickster uses bisociation when inverting problems. "Technical debt slows us down" lives in the frame of code quality. "Slowness creates technical debt" bisociates the speed frame with the quality frame, forcing a connection that reveals the causal arrow might point the other direction.

Bisociation is also the mechanism behind productive absurdity. An absurd proposal connects the problem frame with an unrelated frame (pricing, physical constraints, extreme elimination), and the connection reveals structure that was invisible within the original frame alone.

## Paradoxical Intervention

From family systems therapy (Watzlawick, Weakland, Fisch, "Change," 1974), paradoxical intervention prescribes the symptom. Instead of trying to stop an unwanted behavior or pattern, the therapist asks the client to do it more, do it deliberately, or do it at a scheduled time.

The paradox works because the "problem" often persists because of the attempts to solve it. Insomnia gets worse the harder you try to sleep. Organizational dysfunction gets worse the more you try to control it. By prescribing the symptom, the paradoxical intervention breaks the problem-solution cycle.

The trickster uses this technique when proposing that the "problem" might be the solution. "You have too many meetings" might be addressed not by reducing meetings but by making meetings so costly (in visible terms) that the system self-corrects. The paradox reveals the hidden feedback loop.

## Reframing Theory

Reframing (Watzlawick, "The Language of Change," 1978) changes the conceptual or emotional context in which a situation is experienced. The facts remain the same; the meaning changes. A "stubborn" child becomes a "persistent" child. A "failed project" becomes a "learning investment."

The trickster's frame_break output is a structured reframe. It names the current game (the current frame), the new game (the reframe), the specific rule that changed, and what opens up. The power of reframing is that it does not require new information, only a new way of organizing existing information.

Effective reframes have three qualities. They must be plausible (the user can see how the new frame fits the facts). They must be actionable (the new frame suggests different options than the old one). They must be non-coercive (the user freely chooses whether to adopt the new frame).

## Creative Destruction (Schumpeter)

Joseph Schumpeter's "creative destruction" (1942) describes how new economic structures emerge from the destruction of old ones. The trickster borrows this principle at the cognitive level: new frames of thinking emerge from the destruction of old frames. You cannot build the new frame without first breaking the old one.

The trickster's method is creative destruction in miniature. Surface the invisible assumptions (identify the old structure). Violate them through inversion and absurdity (destroy the old structure). Synthesize new options from the rubble (create the new structure). The key insight from Schumpeter is that destruction alone is not enough. The creative part, building something better from the pieces, is what distinguishes the trickster from mere chaos.

## Frame Analysis (Goffman)

Erving Goffman's frame analysis (1974) describes how people organize experience through cognitive frames. Frames determine what is relevant, what is ignored, what options exist, and what is unthinkable. The trickster's core method is frame detection and frame violation.

Goffman identified that frames are maintained socially: people reinforce each other's frames through shared assumptions, language, and behavior. This is why organizational stuck-ness is so persistent. Everyone is operating within the same frame, and the frame is maintained by consensus. The Jester personality variant is particularly attuned to this social dimension of frame maintenance.
