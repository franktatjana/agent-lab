# Glossary and Resources

Core terminology and external resources for the Trickster Agent's methodology.

## When to Load

Always available as baseline reference. Load when defining terms, explaining the methodology to users, or when the agent needs to reference specific techniques by name.

## Glossary

**Invisible Assumption**: A belief, rule, or constraint that the user has accepted without noticing they had a choice. Invisible assumptions feel like facts ("that's just how it works") but are actually design decisions that could be made differently. The trickster's first and most important job is to make these visible.

**Inversion**: Deliberately flipping a problem, assumption, or causal claim to its opposite. Not because the opposite is true, but because the flip reveals hidden structure in the original framing. "Technical debt slows us down" inverted to "Slowness creates technical debt" reveals a causal question that was previously invisible.

**Frame**: The cognitive structure that determines which options are visible and which are invisible. A frame is not a belief but a lens: it shapes what you can see, often without your awareness. "Build or buy" is a frame. "Compose from pieces" is a different frame for the same situation.

**Frame Break**: The act of rejecting the current frame entirely and proposing a different one. Distinguished from reframing (which adjusts the frame) by its scope: a frame break changes the game rather than changing how you play the current game.

**Productive Absurdity**: A deliberately impossible or impractical proposal designed to violate a specific invisible assumption. The absurdity is not the answer. It is a vehicle for reaching the real insight, which would be invisible from within the current frame. Every productive absurdity must contain an extractable real insight; absurdity without insight is noise.

**Bisociation**: Arthur Koestler's term for connecting two previously unrelated frames of reference. Where association connects ideas within a frame, bisociation connects ideas across frames. The creative shock of connecting unrelated domains often reveals structure that neither domain could reveal alone.

**Paradoxical Intervention**: Prescribing the symptom instead of trying to eliminate it. From family systems therapy. The trickster uses this when the "solution" is actually sustaining the problem: stop trying to fix it and instead amplify it, revealing the hidden feedback loop.

**Lateral Thinking**: Edward de Bono's term for reasoning that moves sideways rather than vertically. Lateral thinking does not build on the previous step; it jumps to a different position and sees the problem from there. The trickster's inversions and absurd proposals are forms of lateral thinking.

**Reframe**: Changing the conceptual or emotional context of a situation without changing the facts. A "failure" reframed as a "learning investment" suggests different next steps. The trickster's reframes are always actionable: they must open new options, not just change feelings.

**Creative Destruction**: Schumpeter's economic concept applied to cognition. New frames emerge from the destruction of old frames. The trickster breaks frames in order to build better ones. Destruction without creation is vandalism; creation without destruction is decoration.

**Trickster Counsel**: The synthesis output where the trickster drops the playful method and delivers real wisdom. Written in the active personality voice (Coyote, Jester, or Sage-Fool), the counsel should be quotable, memorable, and genuinely useful. It is the real advice underneath the chaos.

**Serious Options**: The actionable output of a trickster session. Options that were invisible before the reframe, now made visible and concrete enough to act on. Each serious option must pass the "Monday morning test": could the user start working on this next Monday?

**False Dilemma**: A framing that presents only two options when more exist. "Build or buy," "pivot or double down," "speed or quality." The trickster specializes in revealing false dilemmas and opening the option space beyond them.

**Constraint Testing**: The trickster's practice of questioning whether stated constraints are genuine or assumed. "We can't do X" often means "we haven't tried X" or "we assumed X is impossible." The trickster tests constraints by violating them in thought experiments and observing whether the constraint is structural (physics, law) or assumed (habit, convention, fear).

## External Resources

**Books:**
- Edward de Bono, "Lateral Thinking: Creativity Step by Step" (1970)
- Edward de Bono, "Po: Beyond Yes and No" (1972)
- Arthur Koestler, "The Act of Creation" (1964)
- Paul Watzlawick et al., "Change: Principles of Problem Formation and Problem Resolution" (1974)
- George Lakoff and Mark Johnson, "Metaphors We Live By" (1980)
- Roger von Oech, "A Whack on the Side of the Head" (1983)

**Online:**
- Wikipedia: Lateral Thinking
- Wikipedia: Trickster (mythology)
- Farnam Street: Mental Models for creative problem-solving
- Edward de Bono Foundation: Lateral Thinking tools and techniques

**Related Frameworks:**
- Six Thinking Hats: Green Hat thinking overlaps with the trickster's generative phase; the trickster goes further by breaking frames rather than thinking within them
- Design Thinking: The trickster's reframes can feed into design thinking's ideation phase with a broader solution space
- Pre-mortem: Shares the trickster's interest in "what could go wrong" but works within the current frame rather than breaking it
- Devil's Advocate: Argues against a position within the current frame; the trickster changes the frame entirely
- TRIZ (Theory of Inventive Problem Solving): Shares the trickster's interest in contradiction resolution and assumption violation, applied specifically to engineering and invention
