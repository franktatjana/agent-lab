# Surface Assumptions

Identify the invisible rules, constraints, and assumptions the user has accepted without questioning. These are the walls of the box they cannot see from inside. Every stuck situation is held in place by assumptions that feel like facts. Your job is to find them.

**Input:** {stuck_situation}, {context}, {what_youve_tried}
**Output:** Array of invisible assumptions, each with why_invisible and what_if_violated

---

Read the stuck situation carefully. Listen for the walls. The invisible assumptions are hiding in the words the user chose, the options they considered, the options they did not consider, and the way they framed the problem.

Look for:

1. **Binary frames**: Where the user presents only two options ("we either X or Y"). What third, fourth, fifth option is being excluded, and why?
2. **Unstated "musts"**: Rules the user follows without naming them. "We have to..." or "We can't..." often signals an assumption disguised as a constraint.
3. **Borrowed frames**: Assumptions inherited from the industry, the organization, or conventional wisdom. "That's just how it works in our space."
4. **Temporal assumptions**: "We need to do X before Y" or "It's too late for Z." Are these sequences actually required?
5. **Identity assumptions**: "We're not the kind of company/team/person that does X." Who decided that?
6. **Causal assumptions**: "X causes Y" or "If we do X, Y will happen." Is the causal chain verified or assumed?

For each invisible assumption you surface:
- Name the assumption clearly and specifically
- Explain why it went unquestioned (what makes it feel like a fact rather than a choice)
- State what opens up if this assumption is violated or rejected

Prioritize the assumptions that, if violated, would open the largest new solution space. The most powerful invisible assumptions are often the ones that feel most obviously true.

Do NOT:
- List obvious or surface-level observations as "assumptions"
- Assume the user is foolish for holding these assumptions (they are invisible for good reasons)
- Present your assumption-surfacing as definitive (these are hypotheses to test, not diagnoses)
- Conflate genuine constraints with invisible assumptions without flagging the difference
- Surface more than 5 assumptions (focus on the highest-leverage ones)
