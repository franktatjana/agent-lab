# Trickster Agent

A frame-breaking thinking partner for situations where straightforward approaches have failed. When every option within the current frame looks bad, the Trickster rejects the frame itself, surfaces the invisible assumptions constraining the solution space, and opens paths that rational analysis alone cannot reach.

## The Human Analogy

Every mythology has a trickster: Coyote in Indigenous North American traditions creates through chaos and teaches through surprise. Loki in Norse mythology breaks the gods' plans and, in doing so, reveals what those plans were hiding. Anansi the spider in West African and Caribbean folklore outsmarts stronger opponents by changing the rules of the contest. Hermes in Greek mythology crosses boundaries that other gods respect, carrying messages between worlds that were never meant to talk. The court jester tells the king what no advisor dares say, protected by the disguise of humor.

What these figures share is not rebellion or contrarianism. They change the game rather than argue within it. When every player agrees on the rules and the game still produces only losing moves, the trickster is the one who asks: what if these rules are optional?

## Value Proposition

Most problem-solving works within an accepted frame. Define the problem, generate options, evaluate options, choose. This works well until the frame itself is the problem. When a team is stuck choosing between two bad options, better analysis of those options does not help. What helps is realizing the choice is false, that there are options outside the frame that no one can see because the frame makes them invisible.

The Trickster Agent specializes in exactly this moment. It identifies the assumptions everyone has accepted without noticing, violates those assumptions deliberately, and uses the resulting chaos to surface genuinely useful options that were previously invisible. The method looks like playful disruption. The output is serious, actionable insight.

## How to Use

**When you are stuck between bad options:** Describe the situation and select "Trickster Synthesis" for the full treatment. The agent will surface your invisible assumptions, invert the problem, generate productive absurdity, and land on serious options you had not considered.

**When you suspect a false dilemma:** Use "Break Frame" when someone presents you with an either/or choice and both sides feel wrong. The agent identifies what game is being played and proposes a different game entirely.

**When rational analysis has hit a wall:** Select "Generate Absurd Solutions" when you have analyzed the problem thoroughly and still have no good options. The deliberately absurd proposals are not the answer, but the real insights hiding inside them often are.

**When a problem keeps recurring despite fixes:** Use "Invert Problem" to flip your understanding. Sometimes the "problem" is a symptom and the "solution" you keep applying is actually the cause.

## Multi-Agent Combinations

The Trickster Agent pairs naturally with agents that work on different phases of the thinking process:

- **Design Thinking Agent** takes the serious_options from a trickster session and develops them into structured prototypes with user validation
- **Devil's Advocate Agent** stress-tests a specific serious_option before the user commits, checking whether the new frame has its own blind spots
- **Six Hats Agent** can trigger the Trickster when all six perspectives produce the same stuck answer, suggesting the frame is the problem
- **Pre-Mortem Agent** can hand off to the Trickster when failure scenarios suggest the entire approach needs reframing
- **Decision Decomposer Agent** can escalate when all sub-options in a decomposed decision look bad, signaling a frame problem

## Problem

People get stuck not because they lack intelligence or information, but because they are trapped inside a frame they cannot see. The frame defines which options are visible and which are invisible. Every approach they try operates within the same frame, producing the same unsatisfying results. More analysis, more data, more discussion of the same options only deepens the rut.

The standard advice, "think outside the box," is useless because you cannot see the box from inside it. You need someone to point at the walls.

## Goals

- Surface the invisible assumptions that define the user's current frame
- Reveal non-obvious options through deliberate inversion and productive absurdity
- Break false dilemmas and either/or traps
- Land on genuinely actionable options, not just creative provocation
- Be playful and wise, never cruel or chaotic for its own sake

## Non-Goals

- Making decisions for users
- Providing domain expertise (legal, medical, financial, therapeutic)
- Replacing structured analytical methods (trickster thinking complements them)
- Being contrarian or argumentative (the trickster changes the game, not the position)
- Generating randomness without purpose

## Use Cases

- **Product teams** stuck between "pivot" and "double down" when both feel wrong
- **Leaders** facing organizational dilemmas where every political option has unacceptable costs
- **Engineers** trapped in technical either/or decisions (build vs. buy, monolith vs. microservices)
- **Strategists** whose analysis keeps producing the same unsatisfying conclusions
- **Anyone** who has been told to "think outside the box" and wants actual help doing it

## Ideas

- Assumption audit: run the trickster periodically on your strategy to catch assumptions before they become invisible
- Reverse brainstorm: use "Generate Absurd" as a warm-up exercise before traditional brainstorming
- Frame library: collect the frames the trickster has broken across sessions to build organizational awareness
- Pre-decision trickster check: before committing to a major decision, ask the trickster to find what you cannot see

## Open Questions

- How aggressively should the trickster challenge constraints the user marks as "non-negotiable"? Some non-negotiables are genuine; others are invisible assumptions wearing a costume.
- What is the right balance between playful tone and serious output? Too playful and users do not take the insights seriously. Too serious and the frame-breaking method loses its power.
- Should the agent track which assumption patterns recur across users and domains to build a library of common invisible frames?

## Success Criteria

- Users report seeing options they genuinely could not see before the trickster session
- Invisible assumptions surfaced are non-obvious, not just restatements of the problem
- Every absurd proposal yields a real, extractable insight
- Users act on at least one serious_option from the reframe
- The tone feels like a partnership, not a performance
