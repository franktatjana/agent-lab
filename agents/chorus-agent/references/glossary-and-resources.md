# Glossary and Resources

Core terminology and external resources for the Chorus Agent's multi-perspective analysis methodology.

## When to Load

Always available as baseline reference. Load when defining terms or providing framework context to users.

## Glossary

**Chorus**: A group of context-specific voices generated from the user's situation, modeled on the ancient Greek chorus that provided the audience with perspective the protagonist could not see. Not a fixed set of roles, but characters that emerge from the specific decision and its stakeholders.

**Voice**: A single perspective within the chorus. Each voice has a specific identity (not a generic label), a concrete stake in the outcome, a likely position on the decision, and a distinct speaking style. Voices are generated, not assigned from a template.

**Cast**: The complete set of voices generated for a specific situation. A cast typically contains 3-6 voices. The quality of the cast depends on the specificity of the situation: vague situations produce generic casts.

**Dramaturgical Analysis**: Erving Goffman's framework that treats social situations as performances. People have "front stage" behavior (what they show publicly) and "backstage" behavior (what they think privately). The chorus surfaces both.

**Perspective-Taking**: The cognitive ability to adopt another person's viewpoint. Cognitive perspective-taking models what someone thinks. Affective perspective-taking models what someone feels. The chorus generates voices that operate on both levels.

**Stakeholder Mapping**: The systematic identification of everyone who has an interest in, is affected by, or can influence a decision. The chorus uses stakeholder mapping to generate the cast, then goes further by giving stakeholders a voice and staging their interaction.

**Fault Line**: A fundamental disagreement between voices that cannot be resolved by more information. Fault lines often reflect value conflicts, priority conflicts, or timeline conflicts where both sides are right from their own position.

**Consensus Point**: A place where voices who seem opposed actually share a concern. Unexpected agreement across opposing voices is a strong signal that the shared concern is real.

**Synthesis**: The insight that emerges from the interaction of all voices, something no single voice stated. A synthesis is not a summary or an average of positions. It is an emergent insight visible only when perspectives collide.

**Missing Voice**: A perspective not represented in the cast. The most important blind spot is often not a voice that disagrees, but a voice that was never included. Identifying missing voices is a distinct analytical step.

**Collective Avoidance**: A topic that every voice in the chorus dances around without addressing directly. When all voices avoid the same subject, the avoidance itself is the most important signal.

**Parallel Thinking**: Edward de Bono's method where everyone thinks in the same direction at the same time, rather than arguing opposing positions. The chorus extends this by generating situation-specific directions rather than using fixed categories.

**Steelmanning**: Presenting a position at its strongest before challenging it. Each voice in the chorus should argue its best case, not a weakened version.

## External Resources

**Books:**
- Edward de Bono, "Six Thinking Hats" (1985, revised 2016)
- R. Edward Freeman, "Strategic Management: A Stakeholder Approach" (1984)
- Erving Goffman, "The Presentation of Self in Everyday Life" (1956)
- Robert Selman, "The Growth of Interpersonal Understanding" (1980)
- Adam Galinsky & William Maddux, "Why It Pays to Get Inside the Head of Your Opponent" (2008)

**Online:**
- de Bono Group official framework overview
- Mind Tools: Six Thinking Hats summary
- Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy: empathy and perspective-taking
- Harvard Business Review articles on stakeholder management

**Related Frameworks:**
- Six Thinking Hats: fixed-category parallel thinking (the chorus evolves this into situational voices)
- Pre-mortem: asks "assume this failed, why?" (a focused version of the chorus's future-perspective voice)
- Red Team / Blue Team: adversarial stress-testing (maps to the provocateur personality variant)
- Stakeholder Analysis: systematic identification of affected parties (the chorus uses this as a casting input)
- Devil's Advocate: systematic opposition to the preferred position (a single voice where the chorus provides many)
