# Chorus Agent

A multi-perspective analysis agent that creates a chorus of context-specific characters who comment on the user's situation from perspectives the user cannot easily access alone. Modeled on the ancient Greek chorus, it moves beyond fixed thinking roles (optimist, pessimist) into dramaturgical, situation-driven perspective-taking where the voices emerge from what is actually at stake.

## The Human Analogy

When you face a difficult decision, the most useful conversations happen when someone says "have you thought about how this looks to your customer?" or "what would the person who joins your team next year think of this choice?" The Chorus Agent does this systematically, generating the specific cast of characters who have a stake in your situation and letting their disagreement reveal what you cannot see alone. The voices are not generic debate positions. They are people with names, concerns, and something to lose.

## Value Proposition

Single-perspective analysis is comfortable but blind. Even structured multi-perspective tools like Six Thinking Hats use fixed categories that apply the same lens regardless of context. The Chorus Agent generates perspectives from the situation itself, which means the voices are always relevant, always specific, and always include at least one the user would prefer not to hear. The result is not a balanced summary but a dramatic truth: the insight that emerges only when conflicting perspectives interact.

## How to Use

**Quick perspective check:** Describe a decision you are facing and use the "Cast Voices" skill. The agent generates 3-6 characters who have a stake in the outcome, each with a distinct identity and position.

**Full chorus session:** Use "Synthesize Perspectives" for the complete pipeline: cast, dialogue, synthesis, and blind spots. The chorus speaks, disagrees, and reveals what single-perspective thinking would miss.

**Challenge your assumptions:** Use "Reveal Blind Spots" when you want the agent to go further and identify the perspectives not represented in the cast, the voices nobody thought to include.

**Sensitive decisions:** Choose the Moderator personality when the situation involves interpersonal dynamics or when balanced representation matters more than dramatic tension.

## Multi-Agent Combinations

The Chorus Agent pairs well with other agents for deeper or different analysis.

- **Six Hats Agent** provides structured analytical frameworks when the chorus reveals the user needs systematic decomposition rather than dramatic perspective-taking
- **Difficult Conversations Agent** prepares the user for real conversations when the chorus surfaces interpersonal tensions that need to be addressed face-to-face
- **Corporate Navigator Agent** maps the political landscape when chorus voices reveal organizational dynamics
- **Decision Decomposer Agent** breaks down the decision when the chorus reveals it is actually several decisions masquerading as one
- **Pre-Mortem Agent** stress-tests the preferred option when the chorus reveals consensus is premature

## Problem

People make decisions from their own perspective and call it analysis. Even when they try to consider other viewpoints, they generate the perspectives they expect, not the ones that would challenge them. Fixed-role frameworks (optimist, pessimist, realist) apply the same lenses regardless of context, which means they miss the stakeholders who matter most for this specific decision. The result is decisions that feel well-considered but are actually blind to the perspectives that matter most.

## Goals

- Generate voices that emerge from the specific situation, not from a template
- Give each voice a concrete stake that makes their perspective feel real
- Reveal insights that only emerge from the interaction of perspectives
- Identify the perspectives not represented, the blind spots in the cast itself

## Non-Goals

- Making decisions for users
- Providing domain expertise (legal, medical, financial)
- Replacing real stakeholder conversations
- Psychometric assessment or personality profiling
- Replicating the speech patterns of real named individuals

## Use Cases

- **Architects** deciding between technical approaches who need to hear from the people who will live with the consequences
- **Founders** evaluating pivots who need to hear from customers, investors, employees, and competitors simultaneously
- **Managers** considering reorganizations who need to hear from the team members who will be affected
- **Product teams** prioritizing features who need to hear from the users they are not building for
- **Leaders** preparing for board presentations who need to anticipate which stakeholders will push back and why

## Ideas

- Evolving cast: run the chorus again after new information emerges, see which voices change position
- Cast expansion: add a specific voice the user requests after seeing the initial cast
- Historical chorus: cast voices from different time periods (your company 5 years ago, 5 years from now)
- Adversarial chorus: all voices argue against the user's preferred option to stress-test conviction

## Roadmap

**Phase 1 (Current):** Core chorus with four skills and three personalities

**Phase 2:** Evolving casts, multi-session memory, custom voice injection

**Phase 3:** Team mode with distributed chorus roles, integration with stakeholder mapping tools

## Open Questions

- How many voices is too many? Beyond 6, does the dialogue become noise rather than signal?
- Should the agent ever include a voice that represents the user's own position, or is the user always the protagonist hearing the chorus?
- When the chorus unanimously agrees, is that signal or is the cast too narrow?

## Success Criteria

- Users report discovering at least one perspective they had not considered
- Generated voices feel specific to the situation, not generic or interchangeable
- Synthesis reveals emergent insights, not just a summary of individual voices
- Users seek out real stakeholder conversations after reading the chorus output
