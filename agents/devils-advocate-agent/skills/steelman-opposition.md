# Steelman Opposition

Articulate the strongest version of the opposing view and build the best possible counter-argument. This skill produces intellectual honesty: teams that understand the strongest case against their proposal make better decisions than teams that only hear weak objections.

## Prompts Used

1. **steelman-position** - Articulate the strongest possible version of the proposal
2. **strengthen-counter** - Build the strongest possible counter-argument to the proposal

## Workflow

[Steelman Position] -> strongest version of the proposal -> [Strengthen Counter] -> strongest counter-argument with decisive question and resolution test

## When to Use

- The team is in a debate where both sides have legitimate arguments and need clarity on which position is stronger
- A proposal has opponents and the team wants to understand the opposition's best case, not their worst
- The team is making a "Type 1" (irreversible) decision and needs to see both sides at maximum strength before committing
- Leadership wants a balanced adversarial view: the best case for and the best case against
- The team suspects the opposition has a point but cannot articulate what it is

## What You Get

1. A steelman of the proposal: the strongest possible version of the idea the team is considering
2. A steelman of the opposition: the strongest possible case against the proposal
3. The decisive question: one question that, if the team cannot answer convincingly, suggests revision is needed
4. A resolution test: what experiment or investigation would determine which position is correct

## Example Input

"Our product team wants to rebuild the platform from scratch using a modern architecture (microservices, event-driven). The engineering leadership argues for incremental refactoring of the existing monolith. Both sides have presented their case internally. The CTO needs to decide by end of quarter. Budget is $5M either way."

## Tips

- This skill is particularly valuable for "Type 1" decisions (Bezos's framework): irreversible choices where the cost of being wrong is high and the opportunity to course-correct is limited.
- The resolution test (Kahneman's adversarial collaboration) is the highest-value output. It transforms a debate into a research question. Instead of arguing about who is right, the team runs an experiment.
- Both the steelman and the counter-argument must be genuinely strong. If either side is presented weakly, the exercise produces a biased conclusion. The goal is intellectual honesty, not a predetermined verdict.
