# Attack Assumptions

Systematically challenge each critical assumption with evidence, counter-scenarios, and disconfirming data. This is where the adversarial analysis produces its most actionable output: specific, grounded challenges that the team must address or accept.

## When to Use

After load-bearing assumptions have been identified. This prompt takes the structural assumptions and attacks each one with the goal of finding real weaknesses, not rhetorical points. The attack should be strong enough that, if the team cannot respond, the assumption should be treated as unvalidated rather than confirmed.

## Prompt

You have identified the load-bearing assumptions for this proposal. Your job is to attack each one systematically. The goal is not to destroy the proposal, but to determine whether its foundations can withstand pressure.

**1. Evidence-Based Challenge**
For each load-bearing assumption, search for disconfirming evidence:
- What data contradicts this assumption?
- What analogous situations show this assumption failing?
- What industry base rates suggest this assumption is optimistic?
- What has changed in the environment since this assumption was formed?

**2. Counter-Scenario Construction**
Build a plausible scenario where the assumption fails:
- What specific sequence of events would break this assumption?
- How likely is that sequence given current conditions?
- What early warning signals would appear if the assumption were failing?
- How far along would the plan be before the team noticed?

**3. Adversarial Stress Test**
Apply maximum pressure to the assumption:
- What happens if this assumption is not just wrong, but backwards? (The market is not growing, it is shrinking. The technology is not maturing, it is being replaced.)
- What happens if the assumption holds today but fails in 6 months?
- What happens if the assumption is half-right: true for one segment but false for another?

**4. Verdict per Assumption**
For each assumption attacked, deliver:
- **Attack strength**: weak (assumption likely holds), moderate (assumption is testable but unverified), strong (significant evidence against the assumption)
- **Key vulnerability**: one sentence stating the most damaging challenge
- **What would change your mind**: what evidence would the team need to restore confidence in this assumption?
- **Recommended action**: test it, accept the risk, or restructure the plan to reduce dependency

Present each attack as an intellectual challenge, not a personal one. The team should feel that their assumptions are being rigorously examined, not that they are being criticized for making them.

## Tips

- The best attacks use the team's own data against their assumption. External evidence is useful, but showing that the team's own numbers undermine their assumption is decisive.
- Counter-scenarios should be plausible, not extreme. "What if an asteroid hits" is not an attack on an assumption. "What if the competitor launches a free tier two months before your launch" is.
- Always include "what would change your mind." An unfalsifiable attack is not adversarial analysis, it is obstruction. Every challenge should specify what evidence would resolve it.
- Strong assumptions get stronger through attack. If a load-bearing assumption survives rigorous challenge, the team should feel more confident, not less. That is the point.
