# Strengthen Counter

Build the strongest possible counter-argument to the proposal. This is the adversarial complement to the steelman: if the steelman is the best case for the idea, the counter-argument is the best case against it. A proposal that survives both a genuine steelman and a genuine counter-argument is robust. One that survives neither needs revision.

## When to Use

After the steelman has been articulated. This prompt takes the strongest version of the proposal and constructs the strongest possible case against it. The counter-argument should be intellectually honest: a reasonable person who heard only the counter-argument should genuinely question the proposal. If the counter-argument is weak, the proposal is strong. If the counter-argument is devastating, the proposal has a problem.

## Prompt

You have the steelmanned version of this proposal. Your job is to build the strongest possible counter-argument. Not a list of objections, but a coherent case that, if presented to a neutral decision-maker alongside the steelman, would create genuine doubt.

**1. Find the Central Weakness**
Every proposal, no matter how well-constructed, has a central vulnerability:
- What is the one thing the proposal assumes that is most likely to be wrong?
- What is the one thing the proposal ignores that is most likely to matter?
- What is the one thing the proposal depends on that is most likely to change?
- This is not about finding many weaknesses. It is about finding the one that matters most.

**2. Build the Counter-Narrative**
Construct a coherent argument against the proposal:
- State the counter-thesis: what is the alternative interpretation of the same facts?
- Present the evidence: what data, precedents, or logic supports the counter-position?
- Address the steelman directly: where does the strongest version of the proposal still fail?
- Make it persuasive: a neutral reader should think "that is a reasonable objection" even if they ultimately side with the proposal

**3. Apply Kahneman's Adversarial Collaboration Test**
Design the experiment that would resolve the disagreement:
- What is the specific, testable prediction the proposal makes?
- What is the specific, testable prediction the counter-argument makes?
- What evidence would settle the disagreement?
- Can this test be run before the team commits, or only after?

**4. Deliver the Counter-Argument**
Present as a structured counter-position:
- **Counter-thesis**: one sentence stating the strongest case against the proposal
- **Supporting evidence**: 2-3 specific points that undermine the proposal's foundations
- **The decisive question**: one question that, if the team cannot answer convincingly, suggests the proposal needs revision
- **The resolution test**: what experiment or investigation would determine whether the proposal or the counter-argument is correct

The counter-argument should feel like it was written by the proposal's most intelligent, most informed opponent. Not hostile, but relentless in its logic.

## Tips

- The counter-argument is not a list of everything wrong with the proposal. It is the single strongest case against it. Discipline yourself to find the one argument that does the most damage.
- Adversarial collaboration (Kahneman) is the key technique: frame the disagreement as a hypothesis that can be tested, not a debate that someone wins. This turns the counter-argument from an attack into a research question.
- A good counter-argument strengthens the proposal if the team can answer it. If they cannot answer it, the counter-argument has done its job by surfacing a genuine problem before commitment.
- The "decisive question" is the highest-value output. It gives the team a single, specific thing to investigate that will either confirm or undermine their plan.
