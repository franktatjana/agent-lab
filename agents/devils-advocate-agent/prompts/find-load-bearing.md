# Find Load-Bearing

Identify the 1-2 assumptions that the entire plan rests on. These are the load-bearing assumptions: if they are wrong, everything built on top of them collapses. Every plan has assumptions. Most are decorative. A few are structural. This prompt finds the structural ones.

## When to Use

After the steelman has been articulated and the strongest version of the proposal is established. This prompt cuts through the assumption inventory to find the ones that actually matter. A plan can survive being wrong about many things. It cannot survive being wrong about its load-bearing assumptions.

## Prompt

You have a steelmanned proposal. Your job is to find the 1-2 assumptions that, if wrong, would cause the entire plan to fail. Not the assumptions that would make it harder or more expensive, but the ones that would make it impossible.

**1. Map the Dependency Chain**
Trace the proposal's logic from conclusion to foundation:
- What must be true for the desired outcome to occur?
- What must be true for each intermediate step to work?
- Where does the chain depend on a single link: one customer segment, one technology choice, one key person, one market condition?

**2. Apply the Inversion Test (Munger)**
For each candidate assumption, ask: "If this assumption is wrong, does the plan still work?"
- If yes, it is not load-bearing. It may be important, but the plan survives without it
- If no, it is load-bearing. The plan's existence depends on this assumption being correct
- If "partially," identify what version of the plan survives and what version does not

**3. Check for Hidden Load-Bearing Assumptions**
Teams often know their explicit assumptions. The dangerous ones are implicit:
- What is the team taking for granted that they have never stated?
- What market condition, technology behavior, or organizational capacity is assumed without evidence?
- What would a newcomer to the project find surprising about what the team believes?

**4. Deliver the Load-Bearing Assumptions**
For each load-bearing assumption (1-3 maximum):
- **The assumption**: state it precisely in one sentence
- **Why it is load-bearing**: what collapses if it is wrong
- **Current evidence**: what supports this assumption right now
- **Fragility rating**: how sensitive is it to small changes in conditions?

One to three assumptions maximum. If you identify more than three, you have not found the load-bearing ones yet. You have found a list of important assumptions. Go deeper.

## Tips

- The most dangerous load-bearing assumption is the one the team has never articulated. It lives in the gap between "we assumed" and "we never even thought about it."
- Munger's inversion is the fastest test: "How would I guarantee this plan fails?" The answer points directly to the load-bearing assumptions.
- Teams resist identifying load-bearing assumptions because it feels like admitting vulnerability. Frame it as engineering: every bridge has load-bearing supports. Knowing which ones they are makes the bridge safer, not weaker.
- If the team says "we have no load-bearing assumptions," that is the most dangerous finding of all. Every plan has them. Not knowing what they are means not knowing what can kill you.
