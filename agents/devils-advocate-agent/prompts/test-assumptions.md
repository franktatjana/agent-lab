# Test Assumptions

For each load-bearing assumption, design a test or identify disconfirming evidence. This prompt transforms the assumption audit from an analytical exercise into an action plan. The output is not "here are your risky assumptions" but "here is how to find out if they are true before you commit."

## When to Use

After assumptions have been classified and the load-bearing ones identified. This prompt takes the critical assumptions and designs a test for each: what evidence would confirm or disconfirm this assumption, where can the team find it, and how long would it take? For assumptions that are untestable before commitment, it identifies the earliest possible signal that the assumption is failing.

## Prompt

You have classified assumptions and identified the load-bearing ones. Your job is to design a test for each load-bearing assumption so the team can validate their foundations before committing.

**1. Define the Test**
For each load-bearing assumption, specify:
- **What you are testing**: restate the assumption as a falsifiable hypothesis
- **What would confirm it**: what evidence would increase confidence that this assumption holds?
- **What would disconfirm it**: what evidence would undermine confidence?
- **Where to look**: specific data sources, people to interview, experiments to run, or research to conduct

**2. Design the Minimum Viable Test**
The test should be the smallest, fastest, cheapest investigation that provides a meaningful signal:
- Can the team test this with existing data they already have but have not examined?
- Can the team test this with 5-10 customer conversations or expert interviews?
- Can the team test this with a small pilot or prototype before full commitment?
- What is the minimum sample size or evidence threshold that would change their mind?

**3. Apply the Disconfirmation Standard**
Use Tetlock's superforecasting principle: actively seek evidence that proves you wrong, not evidence that confirms what you believe:
- What would a skeptic look for to disprove this assumption?
- What data would the team ignore because it contradicts their belief?
- What analogous situations show this type of assumption failing?
- If the team can only find confirming evidence, is that because the assumption is strong, or because they are not looking hard enough?

**4. Handle Untestable Assumptions**
For load-bearing assumptions that cannot be tested before commitment:
- **Earliest signal**: what is the first thing the team would notice if this assumption were wrong?
- **Monitoring plan**: how would the team detect this signal during execution?
- **Decision point**: at what milestone should the team formally re-evaluate this assumption?
- **Contingency**: what does the team do if the assumption fails after commitment?

**5. Deliver the Test Plan**
For each load-bearing assumption:
- **Assumption**: one-sentence restatement
- **Test type**: data review, interviews, pilot, prototype, or monitoring
- **Effort required**: hours, days, or weeks
- **Disconfirming evidence to seek**: what specifically would change the team's mind
- **Decision threshold**: what result means "proceed," "revise," or "stop"

The test plan should be executable within the team's pre-commitment timeline. A test that takes 6 months for a decision due in 2 weeks is not a test. It is a delay tactic.

## Tips

- The best assumption tests use existing data the team already has but has not analyzed with fresh eyes. Often the disconfirming evidence is sitting in a spreadsheet nobody has opened in three months.
- Interview-based tests are underused and fast. Five conversations with people who have direct experience can validate or invalidate an assumption in a week.
- Tetlock's disconfirmation principle is the mechanism that makes assumption testing honest rather than confirmatory. Teams naturally seek evidence they are right. The test plan must explicitly direct them toward evidence they are wrong.
- Untestable assumptions are not automatically bad. Some bets cannot be validated before they are placed. The test plan for untestable assumptions focuses on monitoring and contingency, not on pretending the uncertainty does not exist.
