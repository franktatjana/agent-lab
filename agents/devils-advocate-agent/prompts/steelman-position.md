# Steelman Position

The foundational prompt. Before attacking any proposal, articulate the strongest possible version of it. This is always the first prompt in any devil's advocate flow because attacking a weak version of an idea is intellectual dishonesty, and the weaknesses you find in a strong version are the ones that actually matter.

## When to Use

When starting any adversarial analysis. Before finding load-bearing assumptions, simulating adversaries, or building counter-arguments, you need a steelman. This prompt takes a proposal and transforms it from the version the team presented (which may be sloppy, incomplete, or poorly framed) into the best possible version of itself. The vulnerabilities you find in the steelman are real. The vulnerabilities you find in the original may just be presentation failures.

## Prompt

You are conducting a steelmanning exercise. Your job is to articulate the strongest possible version of this proposal before any adversarial analysis begins.

**1. Understand the Core Thesis**
Identify the central claim or bet the proposal is making:
- What is the single most important thing this proposal gets right?
- What genuine problem does it solve, and for whom?
- What evidence or logic supports the core thesis, even if the team presented it poorly?
- Strip away presentation weaknesses and find the real argument underneath

**2. Strengthen the Weak Points**
Take the parts of the proposal that are weakest as presented and make them as strong as possible:
- Where the team was vague, add the specificity they should have included
- Where the logic had gaps, fill them with the best available reasoning
- Where the evidence was thin, identify what evidence would make the case strongest
- Where the framing was poor, reframe in the most compelling way possible

**3. Articulate the Steelman**
Write the strongest version of the proposal in 2-3 sentences:
- State the core thesis as if you were the proposal's most capable advocate
- Include the strongest supporting logic
- Make it genuinely persuasive: a reader should think "that's a reasonable position" even if they ultimately disagree

**4. Name What Makes It Strong**
Identify 2-3 specific strengths that would survive adversarial scrutiny:
- What aspects are well-grounded in evidence or precedent?
- What aspects address a real, verified need?
- What aspects have structural advantages that are hard to replicate?

Deliver the steelman as a genuine attempt to present the best version of the idea. If you cannot articulate a strong version, say so explicitly: some proposals are genuinely weak, and acknowledging that is more honest than manufacturing strength.

## Tips

- The steelman test: would the proposal's author recognize this as a fair, strong representation of their idea? If yes, the steelman is honest. If they would say "that's better than what I said," even better.
- Resist the temptation to steelman sarcastically. "The strongest version of this idea is..." followed by obvious weakness is not steelmanning. It is mockery with extra steps.
- Sometimes the steelman reveals that the proposal is stronger than the team realized. That is a useful finding. Not every devil's advocate session ends with "this idea is flawed."
- The steelman sets the standard for everything that follows. A lazy steelman produces lazy adversarial analysis. A rigorous steelman produces rigorous adversarial analysis.
