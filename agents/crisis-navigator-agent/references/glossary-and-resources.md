# Glossary and Resources

Core terminology and external resources for the Crisis Navigator Agent's crisis management methodology.

## When to Load

Always available as baseline reference. Load when defining terms, grounding analysis in established frameworks, or providing context to users new to structured crisis response.

## Key Terms

**OODA Loop**: Observe-Orient-Decide-Act. John Boyd's decision cycle. The side that cycles faster gains initiative. The Orient phase, where mental models interpret observations, is where most crisis response failures occur.

**Cynefin Framework**: A sense-making framework that classifies problems into five domains (clear, complicated, complex, chaotic, disorder) based on the relationship between cause and effect. Each domain requires a different response strategy.

**Incident Command System (ICS)**: An organizational framework for managing incidents. Provides five functions: Command, Operations, Planning, Logistics, and Finance/Administration. The core principle is unity of command: one person leads the response.

**PACE Planning**: Primary-Alternate-Contingency-Emergency. A communication redundancy framework ensuring stakeholders can be reached through at least four channels. Planned before the crisis, not during it.

**Situation Assessment**: The factual baseline of a crisis: what happened, what's the current state, what's unknown, who's affected. Always the first step before classification or action.

**Severity Classification**: Mapping a crisis to a Cynefin domain to determine the appropriate response type. Different domains require different strategies: best practice (clear), expert analysis (complicated), experimentation (complex), or immediate stabilization (chaotic).

**Action Sequence**: A prioritized, numbered set of actions with named owners, specific timelines, and definitions of done. Actions are ordered by dependency and urgency, and separated into stabilization (stop the bleeding) and remediation (fix the root cause) tracks.

**Communication Brief**: Stakeholder-specific crisis messages drafted for each audience at the appropriate level of detail. Each message includes acknowledgment, impact, action, and timeline for next update.

**Stabilize vs. Resolve**: The distinction between immediate containment (stop the damage from spreading) and root cause remediation (fix the underlying problem). Both tracks can run in parallel, but stabilization takes priority when resources conflict.

**Post-Crisis Debrief**: A structured review conducted after the crisis is resolved. Covers timeline reconstruction, what worked, what failed, root cause analysis, and concrete recommendations with owners. The goal is institutional learning, not blame assignment.

**Incident Commander**: The single person with authority over the entire crisis response. Not a committee, not a shared responsibility. Borrowed from ICS, adapted for organizational crisis management.

**Domain Transition**: The movement of a crisis between Cynefin domains as the response progresses. A chaotic crisis stabilizes into complex, then narrows to complicated as root causes are identified. Recognizing these transitions allows the response strategy to adapt.

**Communication Vacuum**: The period during a crisis when stakeholders receive no information. Stakeholders fill vacuums with assumptions that are invariably worse than reality. Structured communication with committed update times prevents vacuum formation.

## Recommended Reading

**Patterns of Conflict** by John Boyd. The foundational work on OODA Loop and maneuver warfare decision-making. Explains why decision speed under uncertainty matters more than decision accuracy.

**A Leader's Framework for Decision Making** by Dave Snowden and Mary Boone (Harvard Business Review, 2007). The original Cynefin Framework paper. Essential reading for understanding why different crisis types require different response strategies.

**Incident Command System (ICS) Resource Center** by FEMA. The standard organizational framework for incident management. Freely available training materials and reference guides.

**Certain to Win** by Chet Richards. Applies Boyd's OODA Loop to business and organizational decision-making. Bridges military decision theory and corporate crisis response.

**Normal Accidents** by Charles Perrow. Examines how complex systems produce unexpected failures through interactions between components that are individually functioning correctly. Essential context for complex-domain crises.

**The Checklist Manifesto** by Atul Gawande. Demonstrates the value of structured procedures in high-pressure, high-stakes environments. Relevant to building effective crisis runbooks and action sequences.

## Related Agents

**Pre-Mortem Agent**: Receives handoffs after a crisis resolves. The Crisis Navigator handles the active response; the Pre-Mortem Agent helps prevent recurrence by systematically identifying what could go wrong before the next crisis develops.

**Difficult Conversations Agent**: Receives handoffs when a crisis involves interpersonal conflict, blame dynamics, or communication breakdowns between individuals. The Crisis Navigator provides structure; the Difficult Conversations Agent handles the human relationship dimension.

**Leadership Coach Agent**: Receives handoffs when team morale is collapsing under crisis pressure. The Crisis Navigator manages the operational response; the Leadership Coach Agent supports the human side of sustained crisis operations, including burnout, fear, and loss of confidence.
