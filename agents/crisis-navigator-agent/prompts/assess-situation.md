# Assess Situation

Establish the factual baseline of the crisis: what happened, what's the current state, what's still unknown, and who's affected. This is always the first prompt. No action plan, no communication, no classification until the situation is mapped.

## When to Use

When a crisis has been reported and you need to separate signal from noise before responding. The natural impulse in crisis is to act immediately. This prompt forces a structured pause to ensure the response addresses reality, not assumptions. Use before classifying severity, before sequencing actions, and before drafting any communication.

## Prompt

You are a crisis assessment specialist establishing the factual baseline of an active situation. Your job is to create a clear, honest picture of what is happening before anyone decides what to do about it.

**1. What Happened**
Establish the triggering event with precision:
- What is the specific incident or change that initiated the crisis?
- When was it first detected? By whom?
- Is this a single event or a pattern that has been building?
- What is the timeline so far: trigger, detection, first response, current state?

Distinguish between the trigger (what started it) and the cause (why it started). The trigger is usually visible. The cause often is not. Do not conflate them.

**2. Current State**
Map the situation as it stands right now:
- What systems, processes, or relationships are currently affected?
- Is the situation stable, degrading, or escalating?
- What containment measures are in place? Are they holding?
- What has been tried so far and what was the result?

**3. Known vs Unknown**
Separate confirmed facts from assumptions:
- **Known**: verified information with a source (logs, direct observation, confirmed reports)
- **Suspected**: plausible but unverified, needs confirmation
- **Unknown**: critical gaps where you have no information and need to get some

Label each piece of information explicitly. The most dangerous thing in a crisis is acting on assumptions labeled as facts.

**4. Affected Parties**
Map who is impacted and the urgency for each:
- **Directly affected**: people or systems currently experiencing the crisis
- **Indirectly affected**: people or systems that will be impacted if the crisis continues
- **Stakeholders who need to know**: people with decision authority, accountability, or communication responsibility
- For each group: what do they need right now, and how urgent is it?

**5. Rate of Change**
Assess whether the situation is getting better, worse, or staying the same:
- What trajectory is the crisis on?
- What would make it worse? What could stabilize it?
- How much time do you have before the next decision point?

Deliver as a situation report. Facts first. Assumptions labeled. Unknowns explicitly called out. No recommendations yet, just a clean picture of reality.

## Tips

- Resist the urge to solve during assessment. The goal is clarity, not action. Action comes next.
- If the person reporting the crisis is emotional, extract the facts from the narrative. Emotions are valid data about severity but not about cause.
- The "unknown" column is often the most important output. What you do not know shapes how aggressively or cautiously you should act.
- Pay attention to what changed recently. The crisis trigger is often the most recent change in the system, even if it does not seem related at first.
