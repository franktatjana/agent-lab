# Glossary and Resources

Core terminology and external resources for the Pre-Mortem Agent's failure analysis and decision quality methodology.

## When to Load

Always available as baseline reference. Load when defining terms, grounding analysis in established decision science, or providing framework context to users new to pre-mortem methodology.

## Key Terms

**Pre-Mortem**: A technique where the team assumes a project has already failed and works backward to identify why. Developed by Gary Klein. The certainty framing ("it failed") bypasses the optimism bias that weakens traditional risk assessment ("it could fail").

**Prospective Hindsight**: The cognitive effect where imagining an event has already occurred increases the ability to generate explanations for it by approximately 30%. The psychological mechanism that makes pre-mortems more effective than forward-looking risk brainstorming.

**Recognition-Primed Decision (RPD)**: Gary Klein's model of how experts make decisions by recognizing patterns from past experience rather than comparing options analytically. In pre-mortems, RPD drives the identification of failure signatures from analogous situations.

**Failure Signature**: A constellation of conditions that, when present together, predict a specific type of failure. Recognizing a failure signature early allows teams to check whether their plan accounts for the predicted failure mode.

**Regret Minimization Framework**: Jeff Bezos's decision heuristic: project yourself forward and ask what you will regret not having done. In pre-mortems, operationalized as specific "we wish we had..." statements.

**Optimism Bias**: The systematic tendency to overestimate the likelihood of positive outcomes and underestimate negative ones. Pre-mortems specifically target optimism bias through certainty framing.

**Confirmation Bias**: The tendency to search for, interpret, and recall information that confirms pre-existing beliefs. In project planning, manifests as selective citation of favorable data and dismissal of contradictory signals.

**Sunk Cost Fallacy**: The tendency to continue investing in a failing initiative because of resources already committed rather than future expected value. Pre-mortems surface sunk cost reasoning by forcing teams to evaluate the decision as if starting from zero.

**Groupthink**: The drive for consensus in a cohesive group that suppresses dissent and alternative viewpoints. Unanimous approval on complex decisions is a groupthink indicator, not a quality signal.

**Detectability**: How early warning signs appear for a failure mode. A failure mode with high likelihood and silent detectability is the most dangerous combination because the team gets no advance warning.

**Residual Risk**: The risk that remains after mitigation has been applied. Residual risk is what the team consciously accepts, as opposed to risk they failed to identify.

**Blast Radius**: The scope of damage when a failure mode materializes. Containment mitigations limit blast radius without preventing the failure itself.

## Recommended Reading

**Sources of Power** by Gary Klein. The foundational research on how experts make decisions under pressure, including the pre-mortem technique and recognition-primed decision model.

**Thinking, Fast and Slow** by Daniel Kahneman. The definitive work on cognitive biases that distort risk assessment, planning, and decision-making. Essential reading for understanding why smart teams make predictable mistakes.

**Decisive** by Chip and Dan Heath. Practical frameworks for overcoming the four villains of decision-making: narrow framing, confirmation bias, short-term emotion, and overconfidence.

**The Black Swan** by Nassim Nicholas Taleb. Why we systematically underestimate the probability and impact of rare, high-consequence events, and what that means for project planning.

**How Big Things Get Done** by Bent Flyvbjerg and Dan Gardner. Data-driven analysis of why large projects fail, introducing reference class forecasting as a corrective for planning optimism.

**Normal Accidents** by Charles Perrow. How complex, tightly coupled systems produce failures that are inevitable rather than exceptional. Essential for pre-mortems on technical infrastructure and platform projects.

## Related Agents

**Wargaming Agent**: Receives handoffs when the pre-mortem reveals that adversary or competitor response is a critical failure mode requiring dynamic multi-round simulation rather than static analysis.

**Six Hats Agent**: Receives handoffs when the team needs structured multi-perspective exploration of the decision, not just failure modes. Six Hats provides emotional, factual, creative, and process perspectives that complement the pre-mortem's failure focus.

**Why Agent**: Receives handoffs when the pre-mortem surfaces a current problem whose root cause is unclear. The Why Agent's structured investigation (Five Whys, causal analysis) goes deeper into existing issues than the pre-mortem's forward-looking analysis.
