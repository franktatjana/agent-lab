# Regret Projection

Project forward 6-12 months from the decision point. Identify what the team will wish they had considered, investigated, or acted on before committing resources.

## When to Use

After a failure narrative has established the failure context. This prompt shifts from "what went wrong" to "what will you wish you had done differently." The regret projection is particularly powerful for decisions that are difficult to reverse: large budget commitments, organizational restructuring, market entries, or technology platform choices. Bezos's Regret Minimization Framework provides the anchor: minimize the number of things your future self will regret.

## Prompt

You are projecting forward from the current decision point. It is now 6-12 months later. The team is looking back. Your job is to identify what they regret not considering.

**1. The Decision Snapshot**
Capture the decision as it stands right now:
- What is being committed (budget, people, reputation, time)?
- What alternatives were considered and rejected?
- What information was available but not gathered?
- What conversations were avoided?

**2. The Regret Inventory**
Generate 3-5 specific regret statements. Each follows this structure:
- "We wish we had [action] before [commitment], because [consequence that is now visible]"
- Example: "We wish we had interviewed 10 customers in the target segment before committing $2M to development, because the feature they actually needed was different from what we assumed"

Categories to explore:
- **Information regret**: what data was available but nobody collected?
- **Conversation regret**: what difficult discussion was avoided because it was uncomfortable?
- **Alternative regret**: what option was dismissed too quickly because the team was anchored on the current plan?
- **Timing regret**: what would have been easy to do 3 months ago that is now expensive or impossible?
- **People regret**: who should have been consulted, retained, or hired before the commitment was made?

**3. The Reversibility Test**
For each regret item, assess:
- **Reversible or irreversible**: can the team course-correct if this regret materializes, or is the decision locked in?
- **Cost of delay**: what does it cost to investigate this now vs. discovering it later?
- **Regret intensity**: on a scale of "annoying" to "career-defining," how much will this hurt?

**4. The "One More Week" Question**
If the team had one more week before committing, what single investigation or conversation would reduce future regret the most? This is the highest-leverage pre-commitment action.

Deliver the regret inventory as specific, concrete statements. Abstract regrets ("we wish we had planned better") are worthless. Specific regrets ("we wish we had validated the API integration timeline with the vendor before signing the contract") change behavior.

## Tips

- Irreversible decisions deserve the most pre-mortem attention. Reversible decisions can be corrected. Irreversible ones compound.
- People regret is consistently underweighted. Teams focus on technology, market, and budget risks while treating key-person dependencies as stable.
- The "one more week" question often reveals the single highest-value action. If the team cannot answer it, they have not thought carefully enough about what they do not know.
- Regret projection works because it engages loss aversion. People are more motivated to avoid regret than to pursue gain.
