# Plan Approach

Choose the right framework and structure for the conversation based on the situation assessment.

## When to Use

After assess-situation has mapped the terrain. This prompt selects the framework and builds the conversation structure.

## Prompt

Based on the situation assessment, design the conversation approach.

**1. Framework Selection**
Choose the most appropriate framework for this situation:
- **Three Conversations** (Stone/Patton/Heen): Best when both parties have different stories and identity is at stake
- **NVC / OFNR** (Rosenberg): Best when you need to express a need without triggering defensiveness
- **STATE** (Crucial Conversations): Best when you need to share a difficult truth with someone who has power over you
- **DEAR MAN** (DBT): Best when you need to assert a boundary or make a specific request

Explain why this framework fits and what it will help with.

**2. Conversation Structure**
- Opening: How will you set the tone and name the topic?
- Middle: What are the 2-3 key points to cover?
- Closing: What outcome are you aiming for? What does "good enough" look like?

**3. Sequencing**
- What to say first (build safety, then introduce the topic)
- What to hold back until they've spoken (listen before solving)
- What to avoid entirely (blame language, absolutes, bringing up old issues)

**4. Environment**
- Where: private, neutral space
- When: not rushed, not Friday afternoon, not right after a stressor
- How: in person if possible, video if not, never over text or email for the first conversation

Deliver as a clear conversation plan with rationale for each decision.

## Tips

- If the user wants to "just get it over with," push back. Preparation is the difference between a conversation that damages and one that repairs.
- The framework isn't a script. It's a structure to fall back on when emotions run high.
