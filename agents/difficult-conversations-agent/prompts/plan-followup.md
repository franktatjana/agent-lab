# Plan Follow-Up

Design the post-conversation follow-up to maintain the relationship and address unresolved items.

## When to Use

After a difficult conversation has happened (or as part of preparation, to plan the follow-up in advance).

## Prompt

The conversation doesn't end when you leave the room. Design a follow-up plan.

**1. Immediate (Within 24 Hours)**
- Send a brief message acknowledging the conversation: "Thank you for having that conversation with me. I know it wasn't easy."
- Summarize any agreements or next steps in writing
- Don't apologize for having the conversation (you can apologize for impact, not for the act of being honest)

**2. Short-Term (1-2 Weeks)**
- Check in informally: "How are you feeling about our conversation?"
- Deliver on any commitments you made
- Watch for signs of withdrawal or resentment and address them early
- If the conversation didn't go well: "I've been thinking about our conversation. I don't think I handled [specific thing] well. Can we revisit that?"

**3. Long-Term Repair**
- Rebuild trust through consistent behavior, not grand gestures
- Reference the conversation naturally when relevant: "This connects to what we discussed"
- Acknowledge progress: "I've noticed [specific positive change]. I appreciate it."

**4. Unresolved Items**
- What was left unsaid or unfinished?
- Does a second conversation need to be scheduled?
- Are there structural changes (process, roles, expectations) that need to happen alongside the interpersonal repair?

Deliver as a follow-up timeline with specific actions and check-in points.

## Tips

- The follow-up is where most people fail. The conversation took courage; the follow-up takes discipline.
- If you said you'd do something, do it. Broken follow-through destroys whatever trust the conversation built.
