# Difficult Conversations Frameworks

IP Notes: Difficult Conversations (Stone, Patton, Heen) is a published book from the Harvard Negotiation Project. Crucial Conversations (Patterson et al.) is a commercially published framework. Nonviolent Communication (Rosenberg) is published academic work. DEAR MAN originates from DBT (Dialectical Behavior Therapy), a widely documented clinical framework. All referenced for educational purposes.

## The Three Conversations Model

**Source:** Stone, Patton, Heen, "Difficult Conversations: How to Discuss What Matters Most" (Harvard Negotiation Project)

Every difficult conversation is actually three conversations happening simultaneously:

**1. The "What Happened?" Conversation**
Each person has a different story about what happened. Most people assume their story is the truth. The shift: move from certainty ("Here's what happened") to curiosity ("Here's how I see it. How do you see it?"). Separate impact from intent. They may not have intended to hurt you, but the impact was real. Both things can be true.

**2. The Feelings Conversation**
Feelings are at the heart of difficult conversations, even when they're hidden. Unexpressed feelings leak into the conversation as tone, body language, and subtext. The shift: name emotions directly. "I felt overlooked" is more productive than sighing and hoping they notice.

**3. The Identity Conversation**
The conversation threatens how one or both people see themselves. "Am I competent? Am I a good person? Am I worthy of respect?" When identity is at stake, people stop listening and start defending. The shift: acknowledge the identity threat. "I know this might feel like a reflection on your work. It's not. Here's what it is."

## Nonviolent Communication (NVC / OFNR)

**Source:** Marshall Rosenberg, "Nonviolent Communication: A Language of Life"

A four-step framework for expressing needs without triggering defensiveness:

- **Observation**: State what happened without evaluation. "When I see/hear [specific behavior]..."
- **Feeling**: Name your emotion. "I feel [emotion]..."
- **Need**: Connect the feeling to an unmet need. "Because I need [need]..."
- **Request**: Make a specific, actionable request. "Would you be willing to [specific action]?"

The power of NVC is in the separation. Most people blend observation with evaluation ("You're always late" vs. "You arrived after the meeting started three times this month") and feeling with judgment ("I feel like you don't care" vs. "I feel frustrated").

## Crucial Conversations: STATE Method

**Source:** Patterson, Grenny, McMillan, Switzler, "Crucial Conversations"

For sharing difficult truths, especially upward (with managers, executives, or people who have power over you):

- **Share** your facts: start with the least controversial, most objective elements
- **Tell** your story: explain the conclusion you've drawn from those facts
- **Ask** for their path: invite them to share their facts and story
- **Talk** tentatively: "I'm beginning to wonder if..." not "You clearly don't..."
- **Encourage** testing: "I'd love to hear if you see this differently"

The key insight: start with facts, not conclusions. Facts are the least threatening part of the conversation. Once shared, the conclusion often follows naturally.

## DEAR MAN

**Source:** Dialectical Behavior Therapy (Marsha Linehan)

For asserting boundaries or making specific requests:

- **Describe** the situation using facts
- **Express** how you feel about it
- **Assert** what you want or need
- **Reinforce** why it benefits both parties
- **Mindful**: stay focused on the topic, don't get sidetracked
- **Appear confident**: even if you're nervous, maintain eye contact and steady voice
- **Negotiate**: be willing to give to get, find middle ground

DEAR MAN is particularly useful when the user has a history of backing down or people-pleasing in conflict.
