# Scan Environment

The opening prompt. Cast a wide net across the landscape to identify the driving forces that will shape the future. Before you can select critical uncertainties, you need a comprehensive inventory of what is moving, shifting, and emerging across all relevant dimensions.

## When to Use

When starting any scenario planning exercise. Before ranking uncertainties or constructing worlds, you need a map of the forces in play. This prompt takes a strategic context and time horizon and produces a structured scan across political, economic, social, technological, environmental, and legal (PESTEL) dimensions. The scan is deliberately broad because the most important driving forces are often outside the team's primary domain of attention.

## Prompt

You are conducting an environmental scan for scenario planning. Your job is to identify the driving forces that will shape the landscape over the given time horizon. Cast wide before narrowing.

**1. PESTEL Scan**
For each dimension, identify 2-4 driving forces relevant to the strategic context:
- **Political**: government policy shifts, geopolitical tensions, trade relationships, regulatory intent signals
- **Economic**: growth trajectories, interest rate environments, labor market dynamics, capital availability patterns
- **Social**: demographic shifts, workforce expectations, consumer behavior changes, trust dynamics
- **Technological**: emerging capabilities, infrastructure transitions, adoption curves, disruption vectors
- **Environmental**: climate policy, resource constraints, sustainability pressures, physical risk exposures
- **Legal**: regulatory pipelines, compliance burden trajectory, liability framework changes, IP regime shifts

For each driving force:
- State it as a trend with direction ("remote work adoption is accelerating") not as a static fact ("remote work exists")
- Note its current trajectory and momentum
- Flag whether it is well-understood or genuinely uncertain

**2. Domain-Specific Forces**
Beyond PESTEL, identify forces specific to the strategic context:
- Competitive dynamics: who is moving, what are they doing, what capabilities are they building?
- Value chain shifts: where is power concentrating or dispersing?
- Customer/user evolution: how are needs, expectations, and behaviors changing?
- Internal organizational forces: talent, culture, capability gaps, strategic commitments already made

**3. Force Interactions**
Identify 2-3 pairs of forces that interact or amplify each other:
- Which forces reinforce each other (e.g., AI capability growth + labor cost pressure)?
- Which forces create tension (e.g., data regulation tightening + AI model training requirements)?
- Which combinations could produce discontinuities, sudden shifts rather than gradual change?

Deliver as a structured inventory: forces organized by dimension, each with direction, momentum, and uncertainty rating. Flag the force interactions separately, as these often contain the seeds of the most important scenarios.

## Tips

- Breadth first, depth second. The most common failure in environmental scanning is starting too narrow, looking only at the forces the team already monitors. The forces that matter most for scenario planning are often the ones outside the team's usual field of vision.
- Uncertain forces are more valuable than certain ones. A force that everyone agrees on (e.g., "AI will continue to improve") is not useful for building distinct scenarios. A force where reasonable people disagree on direction or speed is a candidate for a critical uncertainty axis.
- Force interactions are where scenarios get interesting. Individual forces produce trends. Force interactions produce discontinuities, phase changes, and the kind of structural shifts that make one scenario genuinely different from another.
- Do not confuse driving forces with events. "A recession" is an event. "Economic growth deceleration driven by interest rate tightening and consumer debt accumulation" is a driving force with a mechanism you can reason about.
