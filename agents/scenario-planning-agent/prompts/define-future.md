# Define Future

Articulate the desired future state clearly enough to backcast from. Backcasting requires a destination before it can plot the route. Most teams have vague aspirations ("we want to be the market leader") rather than specific future states. This prompt forces specificity because you cannot work backward from a destination you have not defined.

## When to Use

When the team wants to backcast, starting from a desired outcome and working backward to identify required steps. Before trace-backward can map the decision path, this prompt must produce a future state that is specific, measurable, and bounded. The quality of the backcast depends entirely on the quality of the future definition. A vague future produces a vague path. A specific future produces actionable milestones.

## Prompt

You are defining the desired future state for a backcasting exercise. Your job is to make the destination concrete enough that someone could recognize it if they arrived there. Aspiration is not enough. Specification is required.

**1. Future State Dimensions**
Define the desired future across multiple dimensions:
- **Market position**: where does the organization sit in its competitive landscape? Market share, segment ownership, geographic presence
- **Capability state**: what can the organization do that it cannot do today? What skills, systems, and processes are in place?
- **Financial state**: what does the P&L look like? Revenue, margins, growth rate, cost structure
- **Organizational state**: how is the organization structured? Headcount, team composition, culture, decision-making speed
- **Customer state**: who are the customers, what is their experience, what is their relationship with the organization?
- **Ecosystem state**: what partnerships, integrations, and platform positions exist?

**2. Specificity Requirements**
For each dimension, the description must be:
- **Measurable**: includes numbers, ranges, or observable conditions. "Strong market position" fails. "Top 3 in the enterprise segment with 15-20% market share" passes
- **Time-bounded**: anchored to the time horizon. "Eventually" is not a future state. "By Q4 of [year]" is
- **Internally consistent**: the dimensions must fit together. A future with 50% market share and a 10-person team is inconsistent. A future with premium pricing and mass-market penetration needs explanation

**3. Success Criteria**
Define how the team would know they have arrived:
- What 3-5 observable outcomes would confirm the desired future has been achieved?
- What would a customer, competitor, or industry analyst say about the organization in this future?
- What would the team celebrate as proof of success?

**4. Boundary Conditions**
Identify what the desired future does NOT include:
- What is explicitly out of scope? (markets not entered, capabilities not built, customers not served)
- What tradeoffs are accepted? (speed over quality, depth over breadth, margin over volume)
- What constraints from today persist into the future? (regulatory, physical, contractual)

Deliver: a 4-6 sentence description of the desired future state with measurable dimensions, success criteria, and boundary conditions. The description should be specific enough that someone could compare reality to the description and determine whether the future has been achieved.

## Tips

- The most common failure is insufficient specificity. "We want to be innovative" is a wish. "We want to launch 3 new product lines in adjacent markets, each generating $10M+ ARR within 18 months of launch, supported by a dedicated innovation team of 15 people with an annual budget of $5M" is a future state you can backcast from.
- Push for tradeoffs. A desired future that includes everything, market leadership AND operational efficiency AND innovation AND customer satisfaction, is either unrealistic or so bland that it does not guide decisions. The tradeoffs reveal what the team actually values and where the tension points in the backcast path will emerge.
- The future state should be aspirational but achievable. If the gap between now and the desired future is so large that no plausible decision path bridges it, the exercise will produce fantasy, not strategy. If the gap is so small that the current trajectory gets there, backcasting is not needed.
- Boundary conditions prevent scope creep during the backcasting step. Without explicit boundaries, the trace-backward prompt will generate steps for everything the team could want, not what it has chosen to pursue.
