# Construct Worlds

Cross the two critical uncertainties to build four distinct scenario narratives. Each quadrant of the 2x2 matrix is a different world with its own internal logic, dynamics, and implications. The goal is not to predict which world will emerge but to make each world vivid enough that the team can test strategy against it.

## When to Use

After the rank-uncertainties prompt has selected two axes. This prompt takes the 2x2 matrix and fills each quadrant with a coherent narrative. Without this step, the matrix is an intellectual exercise. With it, each quadrant becomes an inhabitable world that the team can reason about, argue over, and stress-test plans against.

## Prompt

You have two critical uncertainty axes forming a 2x2 matrix. Your job is to construct four distinct scenario worlds, one per quadrant. Each world must be internally consistent, plausible, and genuinely different from the others.

**1. Quadrant Mapping**
Label each quadrant by its position on the two axes:
- Quadrant 1: Axis A high + Axis B high
- Quadrant 2: Axis A high + Axis B low
- Quadrant 3: Axis A low + Axis B high
- Quadrant 4: Axis A low + Axis B low

**2. World Construction**
For each quadrant, build a narrative world. Each world must include:
- **Setting**: what does this world look like at the end of the time horizon? What has changed from today?
- **Mechanism**: what sequence of events and decisions led to this world? Not a single cause, but a causal chain
- **Winners and losers**: who thrives in this world and who struggles? What capabilities are rewarded?
- **Pace and character**: is this world fast or slow, stable or volatile, optimistic or defensive?
- **Internal logic**: what rules govern this world? What behaviors are rational here that would be irrational today?

**3. Distinctiveness Check**
After constructing all four worlds, verify:
- Can you tell the worlds apart in one sentence each? If two sound similar, one needs reworking
- Does each world have at least one feature that makes the others' strategies suboptimal? If a strategy works equally well in all four, the scenarios are not distinct enough
- Are all four worlds plausible? If one quadrant feels absurd, the axis framing needs adjustment

**4. Background Conditions**
Identify the forces that are common to all four worlds:
- What trends are present regardless of how the uncertainties resolve? (e.g., population aging, digital adoption)
- What constraints apply in all scenarios? (e.g., physics, existing infrastructure, demographic momentum)
- These background conditions ground the scenarios in shared reality and prevent them from becoming science fiction

Deliver four scenario worlds, each as a 4-6 sentence narrative. Include the 2x2 matrix labels for reference. Flag the background conditions that are common across all four. The narratives should be vivid enough to remember and specific enough to test strategy against.

## Tips

- Internal logic is the test of a good scenario. In a well-constructed world, you can predict how actors would behave, what business models would succeed, and what policies would emerge, all following from the conditions set by the two axes. If the team cannot reason about "what would happen in this world if we did X," the scenario is not concrete enough.
- Resist the temptation to make one quadrant the "good" future and another the "bad" future. Every scenario should have opportunities and threats. A world with heavy regulation may be bad for innovation speed but good for incumbents with compliance infrastructure. The goal is variety, not a preference ranking.
- The mechanism matters as much as the outcome. Two worlds can arrive at similar conditions through completely different paths, and the path determines what signposts to watch for. "AI regulation increased" is an outcome. "A series of high-profile AI failures in healthcare led to emergency legislation that expanded to all sectors" is a mechanism.
- Keep the background conditions explicit. Teams often unconsciously assume that current conditions persist in all scenarios. Making the shared assumptions visible prevents one scenario from silently importing conditions from another.
