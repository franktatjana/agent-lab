# Identify Signposts

Define the early warning signals that indicate which scenario is emerging. Scenarios become actionable when teams know what to watch for. Without signposts, scenario planning produces interesting narratives that sit on a shelf. With signposts, it produces a monitoring system that triggers strategic adaptation in real time.

## When to Use

After wind tunneling has revealed how the strategy performs across scenarios. This prompt takes the scenario set and identifies the observable, measurable indicators that would tell the team which future is unfolding. Signposts transform scenarios from a one-time planning exercise into an ongoing strategic radar. The team should be able to check signposts quarterly and know whether their assumptions still hold.

## Prompt

You have four named scenarios and wind tunnel results showing how a strategy performs in each. Your job is to define the early warning signals, the signposts, that indicate which scenario is emerging. Signposts must be observable before the scenario fully materializes, giving the team time to adapt.

**1. Signpost Criteria**
Each signpost must be:
- **Observable**: something the team can actually see, measure, or track. "Market sentiment shifts" is not observable. "Enterprise software deal cycles extend from 90 to 180 days" is observable
- **Leading**: appears before the scenario fully materializes, giving the team a response window. A signpost that appears at the same time as the scenario is a confirmation, not a warning
- **Unambiguous**: points toward one scenario, not multiple. A signpost that is consistent with three of four scenarios does not help the team discriminate between futures
- **Actionable**: when observed, triggers a specific strategic response. If the team sees the signpost and does not know what to do differently, the signpost is informational, not actionable

**2. Signpost Design**
For each scenario, identify 2-3 signposts:
- **Primary signpost**: the earliest and clearest indicator that this scenario is emerging. This is the one the team monitors most actively
- **Confirming signpost**: appears later and confirms the primary signal. Reduces false positive risk
- **Discriminating signpost**: distinguishes this scenario from the one it most closely resembles. This is critical when two scenarios share some conditions but diverge on a key dimension

For each signpost, specify:
- What to observe (the metric, event, or signal)
- Where to observe it (the data source, institution, or market)
- What threshold indicates the scenario is materializing (not just "it increased" but "it crossed X level" or "it persisted for Y months")
- What strategic response it triggers

**3. Signpost Timeline**
Map signposts to a timeline:
- **Near-term (0-6 months)**: signals that could appear soon and would indicate early directional movement
- **Medium-term (6-18 months)**: signals that confirm trajectory and narrow the scenario range
- **Long-term (18+ months)**: signals that confirm which scenario has materialized (by this point, adaptation options may be limited)

The highest-value signposts are near-term ones tied to scenarios where the strategy breaks. These give the team maximum response time for the most critical adaptations.

**4. Monitoring Plan**
Deliver a simple monitoring structure:
- What gets checked and by whom
- How frequently (monthly, quarterly)
- What threshold triggers a strategy review meeting
- Which signpost combinations indicate the team should shift resources or change direction

Deliver: 2-3 signposts per scenario with observation criteria, thresholds, timelines, and triggered responses. The output should be something the team can operationalize as a standing quarterly review item.

## Tips

- The best signposts are the ones the team is not already watching. Teams naturally monitor the metrics in their dashboards. Signposts should direct attention to the signals at the periphery, the ones that would indicate a future the team has not been tracking.
- False positives are a real risk. A single data point is noise. A signpost should require either a sustained trend or a structural change to trigger. Define the threshold clearly so the team does not cry wolf or, worse, ignore genuine signals.
- Discriminating signposts are the most valuable. Two scenarios may look similar in the near term but diverge dramatically in the medium term. The signpost that distinguishes between them early is what gives the team the option to adapt before the divergence becomes irreversible.
- Signposts should be tied to specific strategic responses. "If we see X, we should consider Y" is weak. "If enterprise deal cycles exceed 150 days for two consecutive quarters, activate the mid-market acceleration plan and reallocate 30% of enterprise sales resources" is a standing operating procedure.
