# Rank Uncertainties

From a broad set of driving forces, identify the two that are most critical to the strategic outcome and most genuinely uncertain. These two axes become the foundation of the 2x2 scenario matrix. Selecting the right uncertainties is the most important step in scenario planning, because the axes determine whether the resulting scenarios are genuinely distinct or merely variations of the same theme.

## When to Use

After the scan-environment prompt has produced a driving forces inventory. This prompt takes the broad scan and narrows it to two axes that will generate the most useful scenario set. Without this step, teams either pick the two most obvious forces (producing unimaginative scenarios) or the two most dramatic forces (producing implausible scenarios). The discipline is selecting forces that are both high-impact and genuinely uncertain.

## Prompt

You have a set of driving forces from an environmental scan. Your job is to select the two that will serve as axes for the 2x2 scenario matrix. The right axes produce four genuinely distinct, plausible futures. The wrong axes produce four variations of the same world.

**1. Impact Assessment**
For each driving force, assess its potential impact on the strategic context:
- **High impact**: this force, if it moves in either direction, fundamentally changes the viability of the strategy
- **Medium impact**: this force affects execution speed or resource requirements but does not change whether the strategy works
- **Low impact**: this force is real but peripheral to the strategic question

Keep only the high-impact forces. Medium and low-impact forces become background conditions, present in all scenarios but not axis-defining.

**2. Uncertainty Assessment**
For each high-impact force, assess how genuinely uncertain its direction is:
- **High uncertainty**: reasonable, informed people disagree about which direction this will go. Evidence supports multiple trajectories. The outcome depends on decisions not yet made or events not yet occurred
- **Medium uncertainty**: the direction is probable but the speed and magnitude are unclear
- **Low uncertainty**: the direction is clear, even if the exact timing is not. This is a trend, not an uncertainty

Keep only the forces that score high on both impact and uncertainty. A force that is high-impact but low-uncertainty is not an axis, it is a planning assumption common to all scenarios.

**3. Independence Test**
Check that the two candidate axes are genuinely independent:
- If Axis A moves in one direction, does it force Axis B in a particular direction? If yes, they are correlated, not independent. One of them must be replaced
- If both axes are in the same domain (e.g., both technological), the resulting scenarios will lack variety. Prefer axes from different PESTEL dimensions
- If the two axes produce a quadrant that is internally contradictory (e.g., "high regulation + no regulatory enforcement"), rethink the axis framing

**4. Axis Framing**
For each selected axis, define the two endpoints:
- Use clear, directional language: "AI capability centralizes in 3-5 mega-providers" vs. "AI capability distributes across thousands of specialized models"
- Avoid value-laden framing: not "good regulation" vs. "bad regulation" but "prescriptive compliance regime" vs. "outcome-based regulatory framework"
- Ensure both endpoints are plausible: if one endpoint is clearly more likely, the axis is not genuinely uncertain

Deliver the two axes with: the driving force, why it is high-impact, why it is genuinely uncertain, the two endpoint descriptions, and a one-sentence rationale for why this pair generates the most useful scenario set for the strategic question.

## Tips

- The most common mistake is selecting axes that are correlated. "Economic growth" and "consumer spending" move together, so crossing them produces one plausible quadrant and three unrealistic ones. Test independence before committing.
- Good axes often span different domains. A technological uncertainty crossed with a regulatory uncertainty produces richer scenarios than two technological uncertainties. The cross-domain tension is what makes each quadrant feel like a genuinely different world.
- If the team struggles to identify genuinely uncertain forces, that is a signal. It may mean the team has strong priors that need challenging, or that the time horizon is too short for real uncertainty to exist. Extending the time horizon from 2 years to 5 years often surfaces uncertainties that are invisible at shorter ranges.
- The axis endpoints should be equally plausible and equally developed. If one endpoint gets a paragraph of description and the other gets a sentence, the analysis will unconsciously weight toward the better-described future.
