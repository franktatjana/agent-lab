# Backcast

Start from a desired outcome and work backward to identify the decisions, milestones, and capabilities required at each stage. Backcasting reverses the usual planning direction: instead of asking "what should we do next?" it asks "what must already be true for this future to be possible?" This reversal surfaces long-lead dependencies and sequencing requirements that forward planning systematically misses.

## Prompts Used

1. **define-future** - Articulate the desired future state with measurable dimensions, success criteria, and boundary conditions
2. **trace-backward** - From the desired future, work backward through stages to identify required decisions, investments, and milestones

## Workflow

[Define Future] -> specific, measurable desired future state with success criteria -> [Trace Backward] -> staged backward map with prerequisites, decisions, investments, critical path, and decision calendar

## When to Use

- The team has a vision of where they want to be but has not reverse-engineered the path to get there
- Forward planning has produced a roadmap that feels incremental rather than transformative, backcasting checks whether incremental steps actually reach the destination
- The desired future requires a break from current trends, meaning "more of the same" will not get there
- A major strategic commitment is approaching and the team needs to understand the full chain of prerequisites
- Post-scenario-planning, when the team has identified which scenario they prefer and wants to backcast toward a desired position within that world

## What You Get

1. A specific, measurable desired future state with dimensions across market position, capabilities, financials, organization, customers, and ecosystem
2. Success criteria: 3-5 observable outcomes that confirm the future has been achieved
3. Boundary conditions: what is explicitly out of scope, what tradeoffs are accepted
4. Staged backward map: 3-4 stages from future to present, each with prerequisites, decisions, investments, and evidence markers
5. Critical path identification: bottleneck stages, irreversible decisions, long-lead items, parallel paths
6. Gap analysis: comparison of present state to first required milestone, with feasibility assessment
7. Decision calendar: what must be decided by when, what can be deferred, where the "no later than" deadlines are

## Example Input

"Desired future: within 5 years, we are the leading vertical healthcare SaaS provider for mid-size hospital networks in the US, with $50M ARR, 200+ customers, HITRUST certification, and partnerships with 3 major EHR platforms. Current state: $15M ARR horizontal CRM, 200 employees, no healthcare-specific capabilities, no regulatory certifications."

## Tips

- The quality of the backcast depends entirely on the quality of the future definition. A vague aspiration ("be a market leader") produces a vague path. A specific, measurable future state produces a path with concrete milestones, clear prerequisites, and identifiable gaps. Invest time in the define-future step.
- Long-lead items are the most actionable finding. Capabilities that take 18+ months to build, such as regulatory certifications, domain expertise, platform partnerships, must start now even if they do not feel urgent. The backcast makes the timing visible by working backward from when the capability must be ready.
- The gap analysis is the reality check. If the gap between present and first milestone is too large for the available time, the backcast has revealed that the desired future is unreachable on the current timeline. This is a valuable finding: it is better to know now than to discover at the midpoint that the foundation was never built.
- Backcasting pairs well with scenario planning. After wind tunneling reveals which scenarios favor the strategy, the team can backcast toward the desired future under different scenario conditions to check whether the path changes across worlds. If the first two stages are the same in all scenarios, those steps are robust and should start immediately.
