# Scenario Planning Frameworks

The Scenario Planning Agent draws from five frameworks that address different aspects of building and using alternative futures. Shell Scenario Planning provides the core 2x2 structure. The Schwartz Method contributes the narrative storytelling discipline. The Cone of Plausibility provides the classification system for future probability. Backcasting reverses the planning direction from forward projection to backward construction. Wind Tunneling provides the testing methodology that makes scenarios actionable. Together, they form a system for exploring uncertainty that goes beyond forecasting by making multiple futures concrete, testable, and strategically useful.

## When to Load

Load when explaining methodology to users, when selecting the right approach for a specific strategic question, or when the analysis needs deeper grounding in established foresight practice.

## Shell Scenario Planning (2x2 Matrix)

Shell International pioneered the use of scenario planning as a strategic management tool in the early 1970s. Pierre Wack and Ted Newland developed the approach within Shell's Group Planning division, and the methodology was refined over decades of practice into the 2x2 matrix form that is now the most widely adopted scenario planning structure in business strategy.

The 2x2 matrix works by identifying two critical uncertainties, forces that are both high-impact on the strategic question and genuinely uncertain in their direction, and crossing them to create four distinct quadrants. Each quadrant represents a different combination of how the two uncertainties might resolve, producing four worlds with different conditions, dynamics, and implications.

The power of the 2x2 comes from its constraints. Two uncertainties and four futures force simplicity. Teams that try to build scenarios from five or six uncertainties produce combinatorial explosions of futures that are individually thin and collectively unusable. The 2x2 discipline forces the team to decide which two uncertainties matter most, a decision that is itself strategically valuable because it reveals what the team believes will shape their landscape.

Shell's practice includes several principles that distinguish it from academic scenario work. Scenarios are not predictions: they are structured explorations of what could happen. No scenario is assigned a probability. The set of four is designed to span the space of plausible futures, not to identify the most likely one. The purpose is not to pick the right scenario but to develop strategies that are robust across multiple scenarios.

The methodology also includes wind tunneling, testing existing strategies and decisions against each scenario to identify where they break, where they thrive, and what elements are robust across all four worlds. Wind tunneling is what transforms scenarios from intellectual exercises into decision-support tools.

**Maps to:** The construct-worlds prompt and the build-scenarios skill. The 2x2 matrix is the structural backbone of every scenario planning exercise.

**Source:** Shell International, "Scenarios: An Explorer's Guide" (2008). Pierre Wack, "Scenarios: Uncharted Waters Ahead" (Harvard Business Review, 1985). Published methodology.

## Schwartz Method (The Art of the Long View)

Peter Schwartz, who worked in Shell's scenario planning team before founding Global Business Network (GBN), codified the methodology in "The Art of the Long View" (1991). Schwartz's contribution was translating Shell's institutional practice into a generalizable approach that any organization could adopt, and emphasizing the narrative dimension that makes scenarios memorable and useful.

Schwartz's method follows eight steps: identify the focal question, identify the key forces in the local environment, identify the driving forces in the macro-environment, rank by importance and uncertainty, select scenario logics, flesh out the scenarios, derive implications, and select leading indicators and signposts. The sequence matters because it moves from the specific (the strategic question) to the general (macro forces) and back to the specific (strategy implications).

The narrative emphasis is Schwartz's most distinctive contribution. Scenarios in the Schwartz tradition are not tables of data or lists of conditions. They are stories with characters, plot, causation, and internal logic. The narrative form serves a cognitive purpose: humans process, remember, and reason about stories more effectively than about data tables. A scenario that reads like a story ("In 2030, the EU's Digital Services Act has been replicated across 40 jurisdictions, creating a compliance-first economy where regulatory navigation is the primary competitive advantage") engages causal reasoning in ways that a scenario described as "high regulation, fragmented markets" does not.

Schwartz also emphasized that scenario planning is a learning process, not a product. The value is not the scenarios themselves but the strategic conversation they enable. Teams that go through the process of identifying uncertainties, constructing worlds, and testing strategies develop a shared vocabulary for discussing uncertainty that persists long after the specific scenarios are superseded by events.

**Maps to:** The name-scenarios prompt and the narrative quality standard across all scenario construction. Schwartz's storytelling discipline applies whenever scenarios are being written.

**Source:** Peter Schwartz, "The Art of the Long View: Planning for the Future in an Uncertain World" (1991). Published methodology.

## Cone of Plausibility

The Cone of Plausibility (also called the Futures Cone) is a classification framework from the futures studies discipline that organizes possible futures by their distance from the present trajectory. The framework is attributed to multiple sources in the foresight community, with significant contributions from Joseph Voros and Trevor Hancock, building on Charles Taylor's original work.

The cone defines concentric zones:
- **Projected**: the default future if current trends continue unchanged. This is what most forecasting produces
- **Probable**: futures that are likely given current conditions and known drivers. Forecasters are comfortable here
- **Plausible**: futures that could happen given what we know about how the world works. This is the scenario planning sweet spot
- **Possible**: futures that might happen, including wildcards and discontinuities. Speculative but not impossible
- **Preposterous**: futures that are considered impossible or absurd by current understanding. Occasionally, yesterday's preposterous becomes tomorrow's plausible

For scenario planning, the plausible zone is the target. Projected and probable futures are what traditional planning already covers. Possible and preposterous futures are too speculative for strategy testing. The plausible zone, futures that are consistent with how the world works but do not require extrapolating current trends, is where scenario planning adds the most value.

The cone framework helps teams calibrate their scenarios. If all four scenarios in a 2x2 matrix fall within the projected zone, the exercise has not stretched the team's thinking enough. If any scenario falls into the preposterous zone, it needs grounding. The cone provides a common language for debating whether a scenario is "too conservative" or "too speculative."

**Maps to:** The quality check in construct-worlds and the distinctiveness validation. The cone provides the calibration standard for scenario plausibility.

**Source:** Joseph Voros, "A generic foresight process framework" (Foresight journal, 2003). Trevor Hancock and Clement Bezold, "Possible Futures, Preferable Futures" (Healthcare Forum Journal, 1994). Published research.

## Backcasting

Backcasting was formalized as a planning methodology by John B. Robinson at the University of Waterloo in the 1990s, though the concept has roots in earlier work on normative forecasting. The core idea: instead of projecting forward from the present to estimate what will happen, start from a desired future state and work backward to identify what must happen to get there.

The distinction from forecasting is fundamental. Forecasting asks "given where we are, where are we going?" Backcasting asks "given where we want to be, what path gets us there?" Forecasting is descriptive. Backcasting is prescriptive. This makes backcasting particularly useful for situations where the desired future requires a break from current trends, where "more of the same" does not produce the outcome the team wants.

Backcasting reveals dependencies that forward planning misses. When you work backward from a future state, you discover that achieving condition X at time T requires condition Y to be in place at time T-1, which requires investment Z at time T-2. These cascading prerequisites surface the long-lead-time items that must start early, even when they do not feel urgent. Forward planning tends to front-load the exciting work and defer the foundational work, which is why projects routinely discover at the midpoint that they lack the foundations they need.

The methodology is particularly powerful when combined with scenario planning. After building four scenarios, the team can backcast from the desired future under each scenario to see whether the path to success varies across worlds. If the backcasting path is similar across all scenarios, the strategy is robust. If it diverges significantly, the team needs to identify which path elements to commit to now and which to defer until signposts indicate which scenario is emerging.

**Maps to:** The backcast skill and the define-future and trace-backward prompts. Backcasting provides the reverse-engineering methodology for turning desired outcomes into actionable plans.

**Source:** John B. Robinson, "Energy Backcasting: A Proposed Method of Policy Analysis" (Energy Policy, 1982). John B. Robinson, "Future under glass: A recipe for people who hate to predict" (Futures, 1990). Published methodology.

## Wind Tunneling

Wind tunneling is the scenario planning practice of testing a strategy, plan, or decision against each scenario in the set to assess its robustness. The metaphor comes from aerospace engineering: just as aircraft designs are tested against different wind conditions in a tunnel, strategies are tested against different future conditions in scenarios. The practice was developed within Shell's scenario planning methodology and has become a standard component of strategic foresight.

The wind tunneling process has three outputs. First, a performance assessment per scenario: does the strategy thrive (performs as intended or better), survive (delivers reduced results but remains viable), or break (fails or becomes untenable) in each world? Second, a cross-scenario analysis: which strategy elements are robust (work in all scenarios), which are fragile (depend on a specific scenario), and which are adaptive (can be modified as signals emerge)? Third, a hedge identification: where does the strategy need contingency plans, option-preserving investments, or trigger-based pivots?

The value of wind tunneling is that it converts subjective confidence ("we feel good about this strategy") into structured assessment ("this strategy works in two of four plausible futures"). Teams that wind-tunnel their strategies before committing resources can identify the specific conditions under which their plan fails and either strengthen the plan, build hedges, or adjust their ambition. Teams that skip wind tunneling discover these vulnerabilities in real time, when the cost of adaptation is highest.

Wind tunneling also produces signposts, the observable leading indicators that tell the team which scenario is emerging. Signposts make scenarios operationally useful because they create a monitoring system: the team checks signposts quarterly and knows whether their strategic assumptions still hold or whether adaptation is needed.

**Maps to:** The wind-tunnel skill and the test-strategy and identify-signposts prompts. Wind tunneling is what makes the transition from scenario construction to strategic action.

**Source:** Shell International, "Scenarios: An Explorer's Guide" (2008). Kees van der Heijden, "Scenarios: The Art of Strategic Conversation" (2005). Published methodology.

## Framework Interactions

The five frameworks operate at different stages of the scenario planning process and reinforce each other.

Shell's 2x2 provides the structural discipline: two uncertainties, four futures, no more. Schwartz's narrative method provides the storytelling quality: scenarios as stories, not data tables. The Cone of Plausibility provides calibration: scenarios should be plausible, not projected or preposterous. Backcasting provides reverse-engineering: from desired future to required path. Wind Tunneling provides the testing: strategy against scenarios, producing robustness assessments and signposts.

The sequence matters. Starting with wind tunneling before scenarios are properly constructed produces shallow assessments. Starting with backcasting before the desired future is well-defined produces vague paths. Starting with naming before the worlds have internal logic produces clever labels over empty narratives. The framework sequence, scan, select uncertainties, construct worlds, name, test, identify signposts, is deliberate and each step depends on the quality of the previous one.
