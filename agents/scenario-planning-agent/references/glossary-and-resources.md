# Glossary and Resources

Core terminology and external resources for the Scenario Planning Agent's strategic foresight and futures thinking methodology.

## When to Load

Always available as baseline reference. Load when defining terms, grounding analysis in established foresight practice, or providing framework context to users new to scenario planning.

## Key Terms

**Scenario Planning**: A strategic planning method that constructs multiple plausible futures and tests strategy against them. Distinct from forecasting (which predicts one future) and trend analysis (which extrapolates current data). The purpose is not to predict but to prepare.

**Critical Uncertainty**: A driving force that is both high-impact on the strategic question and genuinely uncertain in its direction. Critical uncertainties become the axes of the 2x2 scenario matrix. A force that is high-impact but certain is a planning assumption, not an uncertainty.

**2x2 Matrix**: The structural framework for scenario planning. Two critical uncertainties form the axes, creating four quadrants, each representing a distinct combination of how the uncertainties might resolve. The 2x2 forces simplicity and prevents combinatorial explosion.

**Driving Forces**: The trends, shifts, and dynamics in the external environment that shape the future landscape. Organized by PESTEL dimensions: political, economic, social, technological, environmental, legal. Driving forces are inputs to uncertainty selection.

**Wind Tunneling**: Testing a strategy or decision against each scenario to assess where it thrives, survives, or breaks. The aerospace metaphor: testing a design against different conditions before committing to production. Wind tunneling converts scenarios from stories into decision-support tools.

**Signposts**: Observable, leading indicators that tell the team which scenario is emerging. Signposts make scenario planning operationally useful by creating a monitoring system that triggers strategic adaptation in real time. A good signpost is observable, leading, unambiguous, and actionable.

**Backcasting**: Working backward from a desired future state to identify the decisions, milestones, and capabilities required at each stage. The reverse of forecasting: instead of projecting forward, it reverse-engineers the path from destination to present.

**Cone of Plausibility**: A classification framework that organizes futures by probability: projected (default trajectory), probable (likely), plausible (could happen), possible (might happen), preposterous (considered impossible). Scenario planning targets the plausible zone.

**Robust Strategy**: A strategy or strategy element that performs well across multiple scenarios, not just the most expected future. Robustness is valued over optimality because an optimal strategy for one scenario may fail catastrophically in others.

**Fragile Strategy Element**: A component of a strategy that depends on a specific scenario to work. Fragile elements need hedging, contingency plans, or trigger-based pivots.

**Adaptive Strategy Element**: A component that can be modified quickly as signals emerge about which scenario is unfolding. Adaptive elements provide strategic flexibility.

**Official Future**: The implicit, unexamined assumption about what the future will look like, usually a continuation of current trends. Scenario planning's primary function is to challenge the official future by making alternatives concrete.

**Prospective Hindsight**: The cognitive effect where imagining an event has already occurred increases the ability to generate explanations for it. The psychological mechanism that makes "imagine this future happened" more effective than "what might happen."

**Failure Signature**: A constellation of conditions that, when present together, predict a specific type of strategic failure. In scenario planning context, a failure signature in a wind tunnel test indicates where the strategy is most vulnerable.

## Recommended Reading

**The Art of the Long View** by Peter Schwartz. The foundational text on scenario planning for business strategy. Schwartz translates Shell's institutional methodology into a step-by-step approach any organization can adopt. Emphasizes scenarios as stories, not forecasts.

**Scenarios: The Art of Strategic Conversation** by Kees van der Heijden. The definitive academic treatment of the Shell scenario planning methodology. Goes deeper than Schwartz into the epistemological foundations of why scenarios work as a strategic tool.

**Scenarios: An Explorer's Guide** by Shell International. The practitioner's handbook from the organization that invented modern scenario planning. Practical, step-by-step guidance on building scenarios, wind tunneling strategies, and identifying signposts.

**The Essence of Scenarios** by Angela Wilkinson and Roland Kupers. Examines why scenarios work as a management tool, drawing on cognitive science, organizational learning, and decades of Shell practice. Bridges theory and practice.

**Strategic Reframing** by Rafael Ramirez and Angela Wilkinson. The Oxford Scenarios Programme approach to strategic foresight. Extends scenario planning beyond Shell's methodology into a broader framework for strategic reframing under uncertainty.

**Thinking in Bets** by Annie Duke. While not a scenario planning text, Duke's framework for decision-making under uncertainty complements scenario planning by emphasizing that good decisions and good outcomes are different things. Relevant to interpreting wind tunnel results.

**Superforecasting** by Philip Tetlock and Dan Gardner. The research on what makes some forecasters better than others. Relevant to understanding the limits of prediction and why scenario planning, which avoids prediction, is valuable when forecasting is unreliable.

## Related Agents

**Wargaming Agent**: Receives handoffs when the scenario analysis reveals that competitive dynamics need move-by-move simulation rather than static scenario assessment. Wargaming adds adversarial reasoning that scenario planning does not provide.

**Strategist Agent (future)**: Receives handoffs when scenario analysis and wind tunneling have identified strategic vulnerabilities and the team needs to make positioning and resource allocation decisions. The Scenario Planning Agent identifies the landscape; the Strategist Agent navigates it.

**Sensemaking Agent**: Receives handoffs when the current situation is unclear before futures can be explored. Scenario planning requires a grounded understanding of the present as its starting point. When the present is ambiguous or contested, sensemaking must precede scenario construction.
