# Consequence Chain

Map first, second, and third-order consequences of each option. Most decisions are evaluated on their immediate effects. The real outcome is determined by what happens after what happens next.

## Prompts Used

1. **decompose-decision** - Break the decision into independent sub-decisions
2. **trace-consequences** - Map first-order (immediate), second-order (6 months), and third-order (18 months) effects for each option

## Workflow

[Decompose Decision] → sub-decision list → [Trace Consequences] → consequence map with first, second, and third-order effects per option, plus comparison across options

## When to Use

- A decision has options that look similar in the short term but you suspect they diverge significantly over 6-18 months
- The team is focused on immediate costs and benefits but has not considered how the market, organization, or stakeholders will adapt to the decision over time
- You need to identify feedback loops, cascading effects, or unintended consequences before they happen
- A previous decision had surprising downstream effects and you want to apply that lesson systematically to the current choice
- Leadership needs to understand not just what happens next, but what happens after what happens next

## What You Get

1. A decomposition of the original decision into independent sub-decisions
2. A consequence map for each option showing first-order (immediate), second-order (6 months), and third-order (18 months) effects
3. Confidence ratings for each consequence level, acknowledging that third-order predictions are inherently uncertain
4. A comparison across options highlighting where they diverge and which has the best second-order profile
5. Identification of feedback loops and compounding effects that could dominate the outcome

## Example Input

"We're considering moving our engineering team from a hub-based office model (3 hubs) to a fully remote setup. The cost savings are clear: $4M/year in real estate. But our CEO is worried about culture and collaboration. What happens after the immediate cost savings?"

## Tips

- Second-order consequences are where this skill earns its keep. First-order effects are usually obvious. The insight lives in "and then what happens?" applied systematically.
- Feedback loops are the most important finding. A consequence that reinforces itself, either positively or negatively, will dominate the outcome within 12-18 months regardless of how small it starts.
- If two options have nearly identical first-order consequences but different second-order profiles, the decision should be based on the second-order analysis, not the first.
- Assign confidence levels honestly. Third-order consequences are speculative by nature. Presenting them as certain predictions undermines the analysis. Present them as "plausible scenarios worth monitoring."
