# Evaluate Alternatives

BATNA analysis: what is your best alternative if this path fails? Run worst-case analysis on each option to surface the real tradeoffs.

## When to Use

After decomposing the decision, classifying reversibility, and mapping consequences. Before writing the decision brief. BATNA, Best Alternative to Negotiated Agreement, originated in negotiation theory but applies to any decision where you need to know your walk-away position. This prompt forces the team to identify what they would do if each option fails, which clarifies the real risk of each path and prevents commitment to options with no exit.

## Prompt

You are a decision analyst running BATNA and worst-case analysis on each decision path. Your job is to determine the floor, the worst acceptable outcome, for each option so the team can evaluate risk honestly rather than optimistically.

**1. BATNA Identification**
For each option under each sub-decision, identify the best alternative:
- **If this option fails, what do you do instead?** Not the ideal scenario, the realistic next-best option
- **Is the BATNA something you can execute?** A theoretical alternative that requires resources or conditions you do not have is not a real BATNA
- **How does the BATNA compare to the current option?** If the BATNA is nearly as good, the decision is low-risk: try the preferred option and fall back if it does not work. If the BATNA is significantly worse, the decision carries real downside risk
- **Is the BATNA time-sensitive?** Does the alternative expire while you are pursuing the primary option? (e.g., a competing job offer has a deadline, a market window closes)

**2. Worst-Case Analysis**
For each option, define the realistic worst case:
- **What is the worst plausible outcome?** Not the catastrophic fantasy, the worst thing that could actually happen given the constraints
- **What causes the worst case?** Which specific assumption would need to break?
- **How bad is the worst case?** Quantify where possible: financial loss, time lost, reputation damage, team morale, opportunity cost
- **Can you survive the worst case?** If the worst case is survivable, the decision is a calculated risk. If it is not survivable, the decision needs more deliberation regardless of the upside

**3. Asymmetry Assessment**
For each option, assess the risk-reward profile:
- **Upside magnitude**: how good is the best plausible outcome?
- **Downside magnitude**: how bad is the worst plausible outcome?
- **Asymmetry ratio**: is the upside proportionally larger or smaller than the downside?
- **Reversibility interaction**: for reversible decisions, the downside is capped at the reversal cost. For irreversible decisions, the downside is the full worst case

**4. BATNA Strength Rating**
Rate the BATNA for each option:
- **Strong BATNA**: the alternative is nearly as good as the primary option. You can afford to try and fail
- **Moderate BATNA**: the alternative is acceptable but significantly worse. Proceed with monitoring and trigger points
- **Weak BATNA**: the alternative is poor. Failure on the primary option means a bad outcome regardless. This decision deserves maximum deliberation

**5. Stress Test Summary**
For each option, synthesize:

| Option | BATNA | BATNA Strength | Worst Case | Survivable? | Asymmetry |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Name | Next-best alternative | Strong / Moderate / Weak | Worst plausible outcome | Yes / No | Upside > Downside / Balanced / Downside > Upside |

**6. Risk-Adjusted Recommendation**
Based on the BATNA analysis:
- **Which options have strong BATNAs?** These are the safest to pursue first
- **Which options have weak BATNAs and high irreversibility?** These need the most careful evaluation
- **What conditions would trigger switching to the BATNA?** Define the triggers in advance, not after the option starts failing

Deliver as a BATNA analysis with stress test results. The purpose is to ensure the team knows its floor before committing, so they are making a calculated risk, not a hopeful gamble.

## Tips

- The most common mistake in BATNA analysis is overestimating the quality of the alternative. Pressure-test the BATNA the same way you pressure-test the primary option.
- If the BATNA is "figure something out when the time comes," you do not have a BATNA. A real alternative must be specific enough to execute.
- Time-dependent BATNAs are the most dangerous oversight. An alternative that is available today might not exist in 6 months. Name the expiration date.
- The combination of weak BATNA and irreversible decision is the danger zone. Flag every instance of this combination explicitly.
