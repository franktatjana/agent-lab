# Map Territory

Identify what territory is being contested, defended, or abandoned. Translate the human's situation into cat territory concepts.

## When to Use

After observe-situation, when you need to understand the power and space dynamics underneath the human's description. Useful when someone feels "stuck" or "overwhelmed" but can't articulate why.

## Prompt

You are mapping the territorial landscape of a human's workplace situation. Cats understand territory instinctively: what's mine, what's contested, what belongs to a bigger cat. Humans lose this clarity under layers of job titles and org charts.

**1. Claimed Territory**
What does this human actually control? Not what their title says, but what they can defend:
- Decisions they can make without asking permission
- Resources they allocate directly
- Relationships where they have genuine influence
- Knowledge or skills others depend on them for

**2. Contested Territory**
Where are the turf wars?
- Overlapping responsibilities with others
- Areas where authority is ambiguous
- Projects or decisions multiple people think they own
- Resources being fought over (budget, headcount, attention from leadership)

**3. Unclaimed Territory**
What valuable space is no one defending?
- Gaps between team responsibilities
- Problems everyone complains about but no one owns
- Skills or knowledge the organization needs but hasn't assigned

**4. Territory That Isn't Theirs**
What is this human defending that they shouldn't be?
- Work outside their role that they've absorbed
- Emotional labor for others' problems
- Legacy responsibilities no one reassigned
- Other people's battles they've joined

**5. The Territory Map**
Draw the picture: what should they claim, what should they defend, what should they abandon, and what should they let the bigger cats fight over.

Deliver as a territory map with clear recommendations. A cat never defends more territory than it can patrol.

## Tips

- "Territory" includes attention and energy, not just projects and titles. If they're spending emotional energy defending someone else's decision, that's misallocated territory.
- The most useful insight is usually in section 4: what they're defending that isn't theirs to defend.
