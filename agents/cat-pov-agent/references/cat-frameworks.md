# Cat-POV Frameworks

All frameworks are original reframings of published behavioral science and workplace psychology research. The cat perspective is an original creative framing designed to make familiar concepts land differently. No proprietary content is referenced or reproduced.

## Territorial Intelligence

Cats understand territory at an instinctive level. They mark boundaries, patrol regularly, defend what matters, and know when to share space versus when to hiss. Territory is not aggression for a cat, it is survival infrastructure.

**Workplace mapping:**

- **Scope ownership**: Know what is yours to deliver. If you cannot name your territory, others will define it for you.
- **Boundary setting**: Mark the edges of your responsibility clearly. Ambiguity invites encroachment.
- **Recognizing turf wars**: When someone repeatedly steps into your space, that is not collaboration, it is a territorial claim. Name it.
- **Knowing when to claim and when to yield**: Not every territory is worth defending. Strategic yielding preserves energy for the fights that matter.
- **Patrol cadence**: Territory you ignore gets taken. Regular check-ins on your key projects, relationships, and stakeholders are not overhead, they are maintenance.

**Behavioral science roots:** Role clarity research (Rizzo, House, Lirtzman), boundary theory in organizational psychology, Job Demands-Resources model (Bakker & Demerouti).

## Selective Attention (Cat Focus)

A cat does not react to every sound, movement, or stimulus in its environment. It categorizes inputs instantly: threat, opportunity, or irrelevant. Irrelevant inputs get no energy, not even the energy of acknowledging them. This is not laziness, it is strategic allocation.

**Signal categorization:**

- **Threat** (engage immediately): Decisions being made about your work without you. Scope changes. Resource reallocation. Performance concerns. Engage fully.
- **Opportunity** (watch, then decide): New projects that align with your goals. Relationships worth investing in. Visibility moments. Monitor, then pounce at the right time.
- **Irrelevant** (ignore completely): Status meetings where you are furniture. FYI email chains. Requests that belong to someone else's territory. Do not attend, do not respond, do not feel guilty.

**Workplace mapping:**

- **Strategic ignorance**: Deliberately choosing not to engage with low-value demands is a skill, not a character flaw.
- **Notification triage**: Not every ping deserves a response. Batch, delay, or ignore based on category.
- **Meeting filter**: Before accepting, ask: does this contain a threat to my territory, an opportunity I should monitor, or neither?

**Behavioral science roots:** Attention management research (Mark, Gonzalez, Harris), selective attention theory, Essentialism (McKeown), deep work principles (Newport).

## Energy Conservation

Cats sleep 16 hours a day. This is not indulgence, it is preparation. A cat that rests well can sprint at full speed, react instantly, and sustain focus during a hunt. Rest is not the reward for good work. Rest is what makes good work possible.

**Workplace mapping:**

- **Deliberate rest**: Schedule recovery the way you schedule meetings. If it is not in the calendar, it will not happen.
- **Energy budgeting**: You have a finite amount of focused attention per day (research suggests 4-6 hours of deep work maximum). Spend it on hunting, not grooming.
- **Hunt-to-maintenance ratio**: Track how much of your day is high-value output (hunting) versus low-value upkeep (grooming, responding, attending). If maintenance exceeds 60%, something is broken.
- **Recovery rituals**: Short breaks between focused blocks are not wasted time. They are the equivalent of a cat repositioning before the next pounce.
- **Saying no to protect energy**: Declining a request is not selfish when the alternative is delivering everything at 40% capacity.

**Behavioral science roots:** Ultradian rhythm research (Rossi), deliberate rest (Pang), decision fatigue (Baumeister), attention restoration theory (Kaplan).

## Comfort-Seeking as Strategy

A cat finds the warmest spot in the house, the best vantage point, the most comfortable surface, and it does so without guilt, negotiation, or justification. This is not softness. This is environmental optimization performed by a creature that refuses to tolerate unnecessary friction.

**Workplace mapping:**

- **Workspace design**: Your physical and digital environment should reduce friction, not create it. Optimize your desk, tools, notification settings, and file organization.
- **Routine optimization**: Identify the recurring annoyances in your day and eliminate them. If something irritates you every morning, fix it once instead of tolerating it forever.
- **Removing friction**: Every unnecessary step between you and your best work is a tax on your energy. Automate, delegate, or delete.
- **Permission to optimize**: You are allowed to make your work life more comfortable. This is not laziness, it is the same impulse that drives process improvement, applied personally.
- **Environment matching**: Cats move to where conditions suit them. If your current role, team, or company consistently works against your strengths, the environment may be the problem, not you.

**Behavioral science roots:** Environmental design and nudge theory (Thaler & Sunstein), flow state research (Csikszentmihalyi), friction reduction in behavioral economics, workspace psychology.

## Social Selectivity

Cats choose whose company to keep. They approach when they want connection and leave when they are done, with no obligation, no guilt, and no performative face time. A cat does not attend a gathering it does not want to be at. A cat does not pretend to enjoy company it finds draining.

**Workplace mapping:**

- **Intentional networking**: Invest in relationships that are genuinely mutual. Quality of connection matters more than quantity of contacts.
- **Leaving low-value social obligations**: Attendance at optional events is optional. If you are present out of guilt rather than interest, you are wasting everyone's time, including your own.
- **Energy-based social budgeting**: Some people energize you, some deplete you. This is data, not judgment. Allocate accordingly.
- **Clean exits**: You can leave a conversation, a meeting, or a social event without elaborate justification. "I need to go" is a complete sentence.
- **Selective availability**: Being reachable to everyone at all times is not a virtue. It is a failure to prioritize.

**Behavioral science roots:** Dunbar's number and relationship maintenance costs (Dunbar), introversion and social energy research (Cain), boundaries research (Cloud & Townsend), networking quality vs. quantity studies (Uzzi & Dunlap).
