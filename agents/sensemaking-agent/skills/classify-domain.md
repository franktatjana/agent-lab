# Classify Domain

Determine whether the situation is clear, complicated, complex, or chaotic using Snowden's Cynefin framework. The classification determines the response approach, and the wrong approach in the wrong domain produces worse outcomes than no approach at all.

## Prompts Used

1. **gather-signals** - Collect and organize signals to understand the situation landscape
2. **classify-cynefin** - Classify the situation using Cynefin framework and recommend domain-appropriate action

## Workflow

[Gather Signals] -> organized signal inventory -> [Classify Cynefin] -> domain classification with evidence, response approach, and misclassification risk

## When to Use

- The team is debating whether to "analyze more" or "just try something," which is often a sign they have not classified the domain
- Experts fundamentally disagree on the diagnosis, which suggests the domain may be complex rather than complicated
- The team's approach keeps failing and they keep doing more of the same, which suggests domain misclassification
- A new situation has emerged and the team needs to determine the right response pattern before investing
- The situation has changed and a previous classification may no longer apply

## What You Get

1. A structured signal inventory providing the evidence base for classification
2. A domain classification (clear, complicated, complex, chaotic, or disorder) with 3-5 specific signals supporting it
3. The appropriate response approach for the classified domain
4. A misclassification risk assessment: what happens if the team treats this as a different domain?
5. A recommended next action matched to the domain

## Example Input

"We're trying to expand into the Japanese market. We've done extensive market research showing demand for our product category. We hired a country manager with 15 years of local experience. But our first two partnership negotiations fell through for reasons neither our team nor our consultants fully understand. Our country manager says 'the market works differently here' but cannot articulate specifically what we're doing wrong. Our US playbook, which worked in the UK and Germany, is producing unpredictable results."

## Tips

- The complicated-complex boundary is the most consequential misclassification. The Japan example above has signs of complexity: experts cannot explain the dynamics, the proven playbook produces unpredictable results, and the system seems to respond differently to the same inputs. Commissioning more market research (complicated-domain response) would likely produce more confidence without more accuracy.
- Mixed-domain situations are common. The technical implementation may be complicated while the organizational change is complex. Classify each component separately rather than forcing the entire situation into one domain.
- Domain classification is not permanent. A crisis (chaotic) stabilizes into complexity, gets analyzed into complicated, and eventually becomes routine (clear). Revisit the classification as the situation evolves.
