# Sensemaking Frameworks

The Sensemaking Agent draws from five frameworks that address different aspects of navigating ambiguity. Weick's Sensemaking provides the core theory of how people construct meaning from confusing signals. Snowden's Cynefin provides domain classification so teams match their approach to the situation type. Wedell-Wedellsborg's Problem Reframing ensures teams are solving the right problem before investing in solutions. Probe-Sense-Respond provides the action model for complex domains. Boyd's OODA Observe Phase provides the discipline of observation before interpretation. Together, they form a system for operating in ambiguity that traditional analytical approaches cannot handle because the situation is too fluid, too contested, or too novel for analysis alone.

## When to Load

Load when explaining methodology to users, when selecting the right approach for a specific situation type, or when the analysis needs deeper grounding in established sensemaking and complexity theory.

## Sensemaking (Weick)

Karl Weick developed sensemaking theory to explain how people in organizations construct plausible interpretations of ambiguous situations. Unlike decision theory, which assumes a known problem with calculable options, sensemaking addresses the earlier question: what is going on here? The theory emerged from studying organizational disasters (Mann Gulch fire, Tenerife airport collision, Bhopal) and high-reliability organizations (aircraft carriers, nuclear power plants, wildland firefighting crews).

Sensemaking has seven properties. It is grounded in identity construction (who I am shapes what I notice). It is retrospective (we can only make sense of events after they have occurred, not while they are happening). It is enactive of sensible environments (we do not discover reality, we participate in creating it through our actions). It is social (meaning is constructed through interaction, not in isolation). It is ongoing (sensemaking never stops, it is interrupted). It is focused on and by extracted cues (small signals trigger large interpretive frameworks). It is driven by plausibility rather than accuracy (people need a story that is good enough to act on, not one that is objectively correct).

The practical implication for the agent is that conflicting narratives are expected, not exceptional. Different stakeholders are engaged in different sensemaking processes, grounded in different identities, extracting different cues, and constructing different plausible stories. The agent's job is to surface these narratives, identify where they converge and diverge, and synthesize a picture that is more complete than any individual's interpretation.

The enactment principle is particularly important. People do not just interpret their environment, they shape it through their interpretations. A team that frames a situation as a "crisis" will take crisis-appropriate actions (cut costs, centralize decision-making, accelerate timelines) which then create crisis conditions regardless of whether the original assessment was accurate. The frame creates the reality it describes. This is why reframing is a sensemaking intervention, not just a cognitive exercise.

**Maps to:** The gather-signals and map-narratives prompts, and the clarify-situation skill. Weick's framework provides the theoretical foundation for treating conflicting signals as data and competing narratives as complementary perspectives.

**Source:** Karl Weick, "Sensemaking in Organizations" (1995). Karl Weick, "The Collapse of Sensemaking in Organizations: The Mann Gulch Disaster" (1993). Karl Weick & Kathleen Sutcliffe, "Managing the Unexpected" (2001). Published academic research.

## Cynefin (Snowden)

Dave Snowden developed the Cynefin framework as a sense-making model for classifying situations into domains that require fundamentally different management approaches. The framework identifies five domains: clear (formerly obvious), complicated, complex, chaotic, and disorder. The central insight is that the relationship between cause and effect differs across domains, and the appropriate response in one domain is counterproductive in another.

In the clear domain, cause and effect are self-evident. Best practice exists. The response pattern is sense-categorize-respond. In the complicated domain, cause and effect are discoverable through analysis or expertise. Good practice exists, but expert judgment is required. The response pattern is sense-analyze-respond. In the complex domain, cause and effect are only coherent in retrospect. No amount of advance analysis will produce the right answer because the system is adaptive: it changes in response to your interventions. The response pattern is probe-sense-respond. In the chaotic domain, there are no perceivable cause-and-effect relationships. The response pattern is act-sense-respond, establishing order before seeking understanding.

Disorder is the fifth domain: the state where you do not know which domain you are in. It is the most dangerous because people default to the response pattern they are most comfortable with regardless of the situation. Analysts default to complicated (more analysis), leaders default to clear (just decide), innovators default to complex (run experiments). The first move in disorder is to decompose the situation into parts and classify each part independently.

The critical boundary for most organizations is between complicated and complex. Complicated problems yield to expertise and analysis. Complex problems do not. The failure mode is applying complicated-domain tools (consultants, models, data analysis) to complex-domain situations. This produces confident answers that are wrong, and the confidence makes them more dangerous than no answer at all.

**Maps to:** The classify-cynefin prompt, and the classify-domain and design-probe skills. Cynefin provides the classification that determines whether the team should analyze, probe, or act.

**Source:** Dave Snowden & Mary Boone, "A Leader's Framework for Decision Making" (Harvard Business Review, 2007). C.F. Kurtz & D.J. Snowden, "The New Dynamics of Strategy: Sense-making in a Complex and Complicated World" (2003). Published framework and research.

## Problem Reframing (Wedell-Wedellsborg)

Thomas Wedell-Wedellsborg's problem reframing methodology addresses a specific failure in organizational decision-making: teams invest significant resources in solving problems that are incorrectly defined. His research, published in Harvard Business Review, found that 85% of executives surveyed agreed that their organizations were bad at problem diagnosis, and 87% agreed that this flaw carried significant costs. The methodology provides a structured approach to checking whether the problem as stated is the right problem to solve.

The core insight is that problem framing is not neutral. The way a problem is described determines the solution space that becomes visible. "How do we increase our market share?" produces competitive strategy. "How do we increase our customer lifetime value?" produces retention and expansion strategy. Same business, different problem, different solutions, different resource allocation. The frame is often set by the first person to describe the situation, and it persists because questioning the frame feels like questioning the person.

Wedell-Wedellsborg identifies several reframing strategies. Look outside the frame: what has been excluded from the problem definition that might be relevant? Rethink the goal: is the stated objective the real objective, or is it a proxy? Examine bright spots: where is the problem not occurring, and what is different there? Look in the mirror: is the problem-holder contributing to the problem? Take their perspective: how does the situation look from other stakeholders' positions?

The methodology operates in three phases: frame (make the current problem definition explicit), reframe (generate alternative problem definitions using the strategies above), and move forward (select the most promising frame and design the investigation or solution approach).

**Maps to:** The challenge-frame prompt and the reframe-problem skill. Wedell-Wedellsborg provides the methodology for questioning the current problem definition and generating genuinely different alternatives.

**Source:** Thomas Wedell-Wedellsborg, "Are You Solving the Right Problems?" (Harvard Business Review, 2017). Thomas Wedell-Wedellsborg, "What's Your Problem?" (2020). Published methodology.

## Probe-Sense-Respond

Probe-Sense-Respond is the action model for complex domains within the Cynefin framework. Where complicated domains reward analysis before action, complex domains reward action before analysis, but the action must be safe to fail. The model has three phases: design and execute multiple small experiments (probes), observe what emerges (sense), and amplify what works while dampening what does not (respond).

The key discipline is designing probes that are safe to fail rather than fail-safe. A fail-safe probe is designed to succeed: it has backup plans, redundancy, and a high success probability. It also has a high cost and teaches the team nothing if it succeeds. A safe-to-fail probe is designed so that failure is informative and affordable. The worst case is tolerable. Multiple safe-to-fail probes run in parallel produce more learning than a single fail-safe pilot that takes six months.

The sensing phase requires active monitoring and the discipline to observe what is actually emerging rather than what the team expected to emerge. Confirmation bias is a particular risk: teams tend to interpret ambiguous probe results as confirming their pre-existing hypothesis. Effective sensing requires pre-defined success and failure signals so that interpretation happens against criteria, not against expectations.

The respond phase requires organizational discipline to amplify emergent successes and dampen emergent failures. Many organizations struggle with this because amplifying a successful probe means committing resources to something that was originally "just an experiment," while dampening a failed probe means admitting that a direction was wrong. Both require organizational courage.

**Maps to:** The design-experiment prompt and the design-probe skill. Probe-Sense-Respond provides the operational framework for designing experiments in complex domains.

**Source:** C.F. Kurtz & D.J. Snowden, "The New Dynamics of Strategy" (2003). Dave Snowden, various presentations and publications on Cynefin practice (2000-present). Published framework.

## OODA Observe Phase (Boyd)

John Boyd's OODA loop (Observe-Orient-Decide-Act) is widely known in military and business strategy, but the Observe phase is consistently the most neglected. Boyd himself emphasized that the loop's value comes not from cycling through it faster than your adversary but from the quality of observation and orientation that feeds the decision. Most organizations shortcut directly from a superficial observation to a decision, skipping the careful observation that would reveal whether the decision is appropriate.

The Observe phase in Boyd's original conception involves far more than data collection. It means taking in the full panorama of the situation: the unfolding interaction between environmental factors, the adversary's actions and intentions, your own actions and their effects, and the observers on all sides. Boyd distinguished between the explicit environment (what is visible and measurable) and the implicit environment (what is inferred, assumed, or hidden). Effective observation engages both.

The application to sensemaking is direct. Teams under pressure skip observation. They see one signal, match it to a familiar pattern, and act. This works in clear domains where the pattern-match is reliable. In complex or chaotic domains, premature pattern-matching produces confident wrong actions. The Sensemaking Agent's discipline of gathering signals before interpreting them, mapping narratives before synthesizing them, and classifying domains before recommending actions is a structured implementation of Boyd's observation discipline.

Boyd's key insight is that the loop is continuous, not sequential. You do not observe once and then decide. You observe, orient, decide, act, and the act changes the environment, which requires new observation. In sensemaking terms, every intervention the team makes based on the situation assessment changes the situation, which means the assessment needs updating. Sensemaking is ongoing, never final.

**Maps to:** The gather-signals prompt and the observation discipline embedded in all skills. Boyd provides the foundational argument for thoroughness in observation before jumping to orientation or action.

**Source:** John Boyd, "Destruction and Creation" (1976). John Boyd, "Patterns of Conflict" (1986). Chet Richards, "Certain to Win: The Strategy of John Boyd, Applied to Business" (2004). Published framework and analysis.

## Framework Interactions

The five frameworks operate at different stages of the sensemaking process and reinforce each other.

Weick's Sensemaking provides the theoretical foundation: ambiguity is normal, conflicting narratives are expected, and the process of constructing meaning is retrospective, social, and ongoing. Boyd's OODA Observe Phase provides the discipline: observe thoroughly before interpreting, and resist the pressure to skip to action. Snowden's Cynefin provides the classification: once the situation is observed, determine what kind of problem you are facing before selecting an approach. Wedell-Wedellsborg's Problem Reframing provides the quality check: before investing in a response, verify that the problem as defined is the right problem. Probe-Sense-Respond provides the action model for the hardest cases: when the domain is complex and analysis alone cannot produce the answer.

The sequence matters. Observing without classifying produces rich data with no actionable direction. Classifying without observing produces confident misdiagnosis. Reframing without observation produces intellectual exercises disconnected from the actual situation. Probing without classification wastes experiments on situations that could have been resolved through analysis.
