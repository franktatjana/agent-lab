# Glossary and Resources

Core terminology and external resources for the Sensemaking Agent's ambiguity clarification, domain classification, and problem reframing methodology.

## When to Load

Always available as baseline reference. Load when defining terms, grounding analysis in established sensemaking and complexity theory, or providing framework context to users new to sensemaking methodology.

## Key Terms

**Sensemaking**: The process by which people construct plausible interpretations of ambiguous situations. Developed by Karl Weick. Not the same as decision-making: sensemaking addresses "what is going on here?" while decision-making addresses "what should we do about it?" Sensemaking precedes decision-making.

**Cynefin**: A sense-making framework developed by Dave Snowden that classifies situations into five domains (clear, complicated, complex, chaotic, disorder) based on the relationship between cause and effect. Pronounced "kuh-NEV-in," a Welsh word meaning habitat or place of multiple belongings.

**Domain Classification**: The act of determining which Cynefin domain a situation belongs to. The classification determines the appropriate response approach. Misclassification (treating complex as complicated, treating chaotic as clear) produces systematically wrong responses.

**Complex Adaptive System**: A system composed of many interacting agents whose behavior is unpredictable at the macro level even when individual agent behavior is understood. Markets, organizations, ecosystems, and social dynamics are complex adaptive systems. Analysis works for the parts but not the whole.

**Safe-to-Fail Probe**: An experiment designed for complex domains where the worst-case outcome is tolerable and informative. Contrasted with fail-safe designs that are expensive and teach nothing when they succeed. Multiple safe-to-fail probes running in parallel is the standard approach to learning in complex domains.

**Problem Reframing**: The practice of questioning the current problem definition and generating alternative problem definitions before investing in solutions. Developed by Thomas Wedell-Wedellsborg. Based on the finding that most organizations are solving the wrong problem because the frame was set early and never questioned.

**Narrative**: A coherent story a stakeholder constructs to make sense of a situation. A narrative selects certain signals, ignores others, and arranges them into a causal sequence. Different stakeholders construct different narratives from the same situation. Narratives are data about how people understand their situation.

**Signal**: A piece of information, data, or observation from the situation. Signals can be primary (direct observation), interpretive (a conclusion drawn from observation), or reported (passed through intermediaries). Signal quality depends on source, recency, and independence.

**Enactment**: Weick's concept that people do not merely interpret their environment but actively create it through their actions. A team that declares a "crisis" begins taking crisis-appropriate actions that create crisis conditions. The frame creates the reality.

**Prospective Hindsight**: The cognitive finding that imagining an event has already occurred increases the ability to generate explanations for it. The psychological mechanism underlying both pre-mortems and retrospective sensemaking exercises.

**Disorder**: The Cynefin domain where you do not know which domain you are in. The most dangerous state because people default to the response pattern they are most comfortable with regardless of whether it fits. The first move in disorder is decomposition: break the situation into parts and classify each part.

**Frame Trap**: A pattern where the problem definition leads teams systematically astray. Common traps include symptom-as-problem (treating effects as causes), solution-as-problem (defining the problem in terms of a preferred solution), and stakeholder-as-problem (attributing structural issues to individuals).

**OODA Loop**: John Boyd's decision cycle: Observe, Orient, Decide, Act. The value is in the quality of observation and orientation, not the speed of the cycle. Most organizations shortcut observation, which degrades everything downstream.

**Probe-Sense-Respond**: The response pattern for complex domains within Cynefin. Design small experiments (probe), observe what emerges (sense), amplify what works and dampen what does not (respond). Contrasted with sense-analyze-respond (complicated) and sense-categorize-respond (clear).

## Recommended Reading

**Sensemaking in Organizations** by Karl Weick. The foundational academic work on how people construct meaning from ambiguous situations. Introduces the seven properties of sensemaking and their implications for organizational action.

**A Leader's Framework for Decision Making** by Dave Snowden and Mary Boone (HBR, 2007). The definitive introduction to the Cynefin framework. Explains the five domains and why matching approach to domain type is more important than any specific management technique.

**Are You Solving the Right Problems?** by Thomas Wedell-Wedellsborg (HBR, 2017). Practical methodology for problem reframing. Based on research showing that 85% of executives believe their organizations are bad at problem diagnosis.

**The New Dynamics of Strategy** by Curt Kurtz and Dave Snowden (2003). The academic foundation for applying complexity theory to organizational strategy. Introduces probe-sense-respond and safe-to-fail experimentation.

**Destruction and Creation** by John Boyd (1976). Boyd's original theoretical paper on the OODA loop. Dense and philosophical, but the foundation for understanding why observation quality matters more than decision speed.

**Managing the Unexpected** by Karl Weick and Kathleen Sutcliffe. How high-reliability organizations maintain awareness in ambiguous, high-stakes environments. Practical application of sensemaking theory to organizational resilience.

## Related Agents

**Why Agent**: Receives handoffs when the sensemaking process has clarified the situation and identified a root cause that needs structured investigation. The Why Agent's Five Whys and causal analysis goes deeper into specific causes than the Sensemaking Agent's broad situation assessment.

**Six Hats Agent**: Receives handoffs when the situation is clear enough for multi-perspective evaluation. Six Hats provides emotional, factual, creative, and process perspectives that complement the Sensemaking Agent's situation clarification.

**Scenario Planning Agent**: Receives handoffs when the situation assessment needs futures testing. The Scenario Planning Agent takes the Sensemaking Agent's domain classification and competing narratives and explores how different futures might unfold.
