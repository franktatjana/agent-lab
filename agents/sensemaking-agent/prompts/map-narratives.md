# Map Narratives

Identify the different stories stakeholders are telling about what's happening. Each narrative represents a mental model, a coherent interpretation that selects some signals and ignores others. Mapping the narratives reveals what each party sees and what they cannot see from their position.

## When to Use

After the gather-signals prompt has produced a structured signal inventory. This prompt takes raw signals and identifies the narrative structures different stakeholders have built around them. Conflicting narratives are not a sign that someone is wrong. They are a sign that different actors are using different frames, selecting different signals, and reaching different conclusions from the same reality. The map reveals the frames.

## Prompt

You have a structured signal inventory from a sensemaking analysis. Your job is to identify the distinct narratives, the coherent stories different stakeholders are telling about what's happening and why.

**1. Narrative Extraction**
For each stakeholder or stakeholder group, reconstruct their narrative:
- **Their story**: in 2-3 sentences, what is happening according to this stakeholder?
- **Selected signals**: which signals from the inventory does this narrative emphasize?
- **Ignored signals**: which signals from the inventory does this narrative exclude or minimize?
- **Causal logic**: what does this stakeholder believe is causing what?
- **Implied action**: if this narrative is correct, what should the organization do?

A narrative is not just a list of facts. It is a story with characters, causation, and a direction. "Revenue is dropping because the market is contracting" and "Revenue is dropping because the product team shipped the wrong features" use the same signal (revenue drop) but construct entirely different causal stories with different implications.

**2. Narrative Comparison**
Compare the narratives side by side:
- **Common ground**: what do all narratives agree on? This is likely factual bedrock
- **Divergence points**: where do the narratives split? What signal or assumption causes the split?
- **Blind spots per narrative**: what does each narrative fail to explain? Which signals does it have to ignore to remain coherent?
- **Self-serving elements**: does any narrative conveniently protect the narrator's position, budget, or reputation?

**3. The Unnamed Narrative**
Look for the story nobody is telling:
- Is there a narrative that fits the signals better than any stakeholder's version but has no advocate?
- Is there a narrative that is true but uncomfortable enough that nobody will claim it?
- Is there a narrative that requires information none of the stakeholders have access to?

The unnamed narrative is often the most important finding. It sits in the gap between stakeholder perspectives and is invisible from any single position.

**4. Narrative Map Output**
Present the narratives as a structured map:
- Each narrative gets a name (not the stakeholder's name, the story's theme)
- Signal coverage: which percentage of known signals does each narrative account for?
- Explanatory power: how many of the observed phenomena does each narrative explain?
- Action implications: if we adopt this narrative, what do we do next?

## Tips

- Name the narratives by their theme, not by who holds them. "Market Contraction Story" and "Product Misfire Story" are more useful than "CFO's View" and "CPO's View" because the labels let you evaluate the stories on their merits.
- The narrative that accounts for the most signals is not necessarily the correct one. A narrative that explains 80% of signals cleanly may be wrong if the 20% it ignores contains the critical data.
- Self-serving narratives are not automatically wrong. They are automatically suspect. A VP who blames market conditions may be correct even if the market narrative also protects their performance review.
- The unnamed narrative exercise is where breakthroughs happen. If you can construct a story that fits the signals better than any stakeholder's version, you have found something valuable.
