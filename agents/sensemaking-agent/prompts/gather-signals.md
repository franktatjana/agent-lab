# Gather Signals

The foundational prompt. Collect, organize, and categorize the conflicting signals, narratives, and data points present in the situation. This is the first prompt in most sensemaking flows because you cannot synthesize what you have not inventoried.

## When to Use

When starting any sensemaking analysis. Before mapping narratives, classifying domains, or designing probes, you need a structured inventory of what the situation looks like from multiple angles. This prompt takes a messy, ambiguous situation and organizes the raw material: what signals are present, where they conflict, what data exists, and what is missing. The discipline is to observe before interpreting.

## Prompt

You are conducting a sensemaking analysis. The situation is ambiguous, and signals are conflicting. Your job is to collect and organize everything that's known before attempting to interpret it.

**1. Signal Inventory**
Catalog every signal, data point, and observation present in the situation. For each signal:
- **Source**: who reported this, when, and under what conditions?
- **Type**: is this a primary observation (someone saw it), an interpretation (someone concluded it), or a report (someone told someone who told you)?
- **Confidence**: how reliable is this signal? Direct measurement, expert judgment, anecdote, or assumption?
- **Timestamp**: when was this signal generated? Is it current or stale?

Separate facts from interpretations. "Revenue dropped 15% in Q3" is a fact. "The market is contracting" is an interpretation layered on top. Both matter, but they are different kinds of signals.

**2. Conflict Mapping**
Identify where signals contradict each other:
- Which data points point in opposite directions?
- Which stakeholders are describing the same events differently?
- Where does the quantitative data conflict with the qualitative narrative?
- What would have to be true for each conflicting signal to be correct?

Do not resolve conflicts at this stage. Map them. The resolution comes later. Premature resolution is the most common sensemaking failure: picking the signal that fits your existing mental model and discarding the rest.

**3. Gap Identification**
Identify what's missing:
- What data would you expect to see in this situation that's absent?
- Which stakeholders have not been heard from?
- What time periods are not represented?
- What domains (financial, technical, organizational, market) have no signal coverage?

Absence of signal is itself a signal. When nobody mentions a known risk, that silence tells you something about the organizational dynamics.

**4. Signal Organization**
Group signals by:
- **Theme**: what clusters emerge when related signals are grouped?
- **Agreement**: where do multiple independent signals point the same direction?
- **Tension**: where do signal clusters point in different directions?

Deliver a structured signal inventory, not an interpretation. The inventory should be complete enough that someone reading it could form their own assessment without needing the original input.

## Tips

- Resist the urge to interpret while collecting. "The sales team is demoralized" is already an interpretation. "Three sales reps resigned in two months, pipeline coverage dropped from 3x to 1.5x, and the VP of Sales described the situation as 'under control'" is signal.
- Second-hand signals are not worthless, but they are less reliable. A customer complaint reported through three layers of management is a different signal than a customer complaint observed directly.
- Missing signals are the hardest to notice. Build a checklist of what you would expect to see and check each box. Empty boxes are findings.
- The inventory is the foundation. A sloppy inventory produces a sloppy synthesis. Spend time here.
