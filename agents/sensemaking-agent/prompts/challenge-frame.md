# Challenge Frame

Question the current problem definition and surface the hidden assumptions that make it feel natural. The most dangerous problem definition is the one nobody thinks to question because it was established early and has been repeated until it became invisible infrastructure.

## When to Use

After signals have been gathered and the current problem framing is available. This prompt is the pivot point in the reframe-problem skill. It takes the team's stated problem definition and stress-tests it: is this the right problem? Are the assumptions embedded in the frame valid? Would different stakeholders frame this problem differently, and if so, what does that reveal? The goal is not to invalidate the current frame but to make its assumptions visible so the team can evaluate them consciously.

## Prompt

You are examining the current problem definition. The team has framed the situation in a specific way. Your job is to make that frame visible, surface its hidden assumptions, and test whether it is the right problem to solve.

**1. Frame Archaeology**
Excavate the current problem definition:
- **Stated problem**: what does the team say the problem is, in their own words?
- **Implied boundaries**: what is inside the problem scope and what has been excluded? Who decided the boundaries?
- **Embedded assumptions**: what must be true for this problem definition to be the right one? List every assumption, especially the ones so obvious nobody states them
- **Origin story**: when was this problem first framed this way? Who framed it? Has the framing survived unchanged while the situation has evolved?

Assumptions that were valid when the problem was first defined may no longer hold. The frame may be stale even if the words have not changed.

**2. Assumption Stress Test**
For each embedded assumption, ask:
- **Evidence check**: what evidence supports this assumption? What evidence contradicts it?
- **Inversion test**: what would the problem look like if this assumption were false?
- **Stakeholder test**: do all stakeholders share this assumption, or do some see the situation differently?
- **Time test**: was this assumption valid when it was made? Is it still valid now?

The stress test should produce 3-5 assumptions that are either unsupported, contradicted, or outdated. These are the fracture lines in the current frame.

**3. Frame Diagnosis**
Based on the archaeology and stress test, assess the current frame:
- **Frame fit**: does the current problem definition account for the majority of signals and narratives, or does it leave major phenomena unexplained?
- **Solution space**: does the current frame point toward actionable solutions, or does it lead to vague recommendations?
- **Stakeholder alignment**: do the people who need to act agree on the problem definition, or are they working from different frames?
- **Frame traps**: is the current frame a known pattern that leads teams astray?

Common frame traps include:
- **Symptom-as-problem**: treating the visible effect as the problem while the cause continues (e.g., "we have a retention problem" when the problem is hiring fit)
- **Solution-masquerading-as-problem**: defining the problem in terms of a preferred solution (e.g., "we need a new CRM" when the problem is sales process discipline)
- **Stakeholder-as-problem**: attributing the situation to individuals when the structure makes the outcome inevitable regardless of who fills the role
- **Scope-creep frame**: defining the problem so broadly that no focused action is possible

**4. Assumption Inventory Output**
Deliver:
- The current frame stated clearly, with its embedded assumptions made explicit
- 3-5 assumptions ranked by how fragile they are (most likely to be wrong first)
- For each fragile assumption, the signal evidence that challenges it
- An overall frame health assessment: is this the right problem, a partial problem, or possibly the wrong problem?

## Tips

- The hardest assumptions to surface are the ones the team treats as natural laws rather than choices. "We need to grow revenue" seems like a fact but is actually a frame. "We need to become profitable" is a different frame that leads to different actions.
- Pay attention to the language. The words the team uses to describe the problem carry assumptions. "We're losing market share" assumes a competitive frame. "Our customers' needs are evolving" assumes a market evolution frame. Same data, different stories, different actions.
- The person who framed the problem usually has the most power in the room. Questioning the frame can feel like questioning the person. Surface this dynamic explicitly.
- A good frame diagnosis produces the raw material for reframing. Do not skip to reframes before the diagnosis is complete.
