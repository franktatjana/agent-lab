# Tabletop Exercise

Test response plans against specific trigger events. Walk through the plan step by step and find where it breaks.

## Prompts Used

1. **set-scenario** - Establish the strategic context and players involved
2. **test-response** - Walk through the response plan against a specific trigger event
3. **debrief** - Review what the exercise revealed and extract adjustments

## Workflow

[Set Scenario] → strategic context → [Test Response] → response walkthrough with decision points and failure modes → [Debrief] → lessons learned and plan improvements

## When to Use

- You have a response plan for a competitive threat but haven't tested it against a specific scenario
- Leadership asks "what would we do if X happens?" and you want a rigorous answer
- Preparing for a known upcoming event (competitor launch, regulatory change, contract renewal) and want to rehearse the response
- A crisis response plan exists on paper but the team has never walked through it
- You want to identify the decision points and resource gaps in your current plans before a real event exposes them

## What You Get

1. A strategic context framing the trigger event within the competitive landscape
2. A step-by-step response walkthrough covering immediate actions, stakeholder communication, and resource mobilization
3. Decision points mapped, showing where the plan requires judgment calls with incomplete information
4. Contingency options for each point where the primary plan might fail
5. A debrief with lessons learned, plan gaps identified, and specific improvements to make before the real event occurs

## Example Input

"Our largest enterprise customer (30% of ARR) just told us they're evaluating Competitor B as part of their annual vendor review. The review process takes 90 days. We have a renewal in 6 months. Walk me through what we do starting now, and tell me where our response plan has holes."

## Tips

- Be specific about the trigger. "A competitor does something bad" is not a testable scenario. "Competitor B announces a 50% price cut targeting our mid-market segment on the same day as our annual user conference" is testable.
- The most common finding is ownership gaps: moments in the response where nobody clearly owns the next action. Identifying these before the real event is the primary value of the exercise.
- Run the full skill including the debrief. The tabletop itself produces findings, but the debrief is where those findings become changes to the actual plan. Skip it and the exercise was entertainment, not preparation.
- After the exercise, assign owners to every plan gap identified. Findings without owners become organizational trivia.
