# After-Action Review

Analyze what happened in a wargame simulation and extract the strategy adjustments that actually change behavior.

## Prompts Used

1. **simulate-round** - The round results being reviewed (input from a completed simulation)
2. **debrief** - Formal after-action review extracting gaps, broken assumptions, and strategy changes

## Workflow

[Simulate Round] → round results → [Debrief] → after-action report with strategy adjustments and key lessons

## When to Use

- A wargame simulation round has completed and you need to turn results into actionable findings
- A real competitive event occurred and you want to analyze it using AAR methodology, comparing what you expected vs. what happened
- You're closing a wargaming session and need to distill multiple rounds into a clear set of takeaways
- Leadership needs a concise summary of what the simulation revealed and what should change
- Your strategy assumptions haven't been stress-tested recently and need a formal review

## What You Get

1. A structured comparison of expected vs. actual outcomes from the simulation
2. Gap analysis classifying every divergence as information gap, analysis gap, or assumption gap
3. An assumption audit showing which beliefs were validated, broken, or left untested
4. Specific strategy adjustments with immediate changes, new contingencies, and monitoring triggers
5. Three to five distilled lessons in the format "what we learned, because of what happened, which means what we do differently"

## Example Input

"We ran a 3-round wargame simulating our enterprise platform launch. In round 1, we expected Competitor A to ignore us for 6 months. Instead, the simulation showed they'd detect our move within weeks through overlapping sales relationships and fast-track a competing feature. By round 3, we'd lost the timing advantage we were counting on. I need to understand what assumptions broke and what to change."

## Tips

- Run the debrief immediately after the simulation, not "next week." The reasoning behind predictions fades fast, and the debrief depends on honest comparison between what you expected and what happened.
- The most valuable output is usually the broken assumptions list, not the strategy adjustments. Adjustments fix one situation. Updated assumptions improve every future decision.
- If the debrief produces zero strategy changes, interrogate why. Either the simulation perfectly validated your approach (unlikely) or you're not being rigorous enough about the gaps.
