# Test Response

Tabletop walkthrough: given a specific trigger event, walk through the response plan step by step. Test whether the plan holds under pressure.

## When to Use

When you have a plan but haven't tested it against a specific scenario. When leadership says "what would we do if Competitor X does Y?" and you want a rigorous answer instead of a confident guess. When you need to find the decision points where a plan might break before reality finds them for you.

## Prompt

You are running a tabletop exercise. A specific trigger event has occurred and you're walking through the response plan in detail, testing every step for feasibility, timing, and failure points.

**1. Trigger Event Definition**
Define the trigger with enough specificity to make the exercise real:
- **What happened?** State the event as a news headline or internal alert. "Competitor A announced..." or "Customer segment X began migrating to..."
- **When did you learn about it?** The gap between event and detection matters
- **What's the immediate impact?** Revenue at risk, customer confusion, talent flight, market narrative shift
- **Severity classification**: is this an annoyance, a material threat, or an existential risk?

**2. Immediate Response (First 24-72 Hours)**
Walk through what happens when the trigger fires:
- **Who gets the call?** Name the role that owns the initial response. If it's unclear, that's finding number one
- **First actions**: what do you do in the first day? Not what you'd like to do, what you can actually do given current resources and decision-making speed
- **Information gathering**: what do you need to know that you don't know yet? How long does it take to get answers?
- **Internal alignment**: how quickly can you get decision-makers aligned? If the answer is "we'd need to schedule a meeting," stress-test that timeline

**3. Stakeholder Communication**
Who needs to know what, and when:
- **Internal teams**: which teams need immediate briefing? What do you tell them vs. what do you hold back until the picture is clearer?
- **Customers**: do customers need proactive communication? What's the message? Who delivers it?
- **Partners**: are partners affected? Do they hear from you or from the market?
- **Board/investors**: does this rise to board-level communication? What's the threshold?
- **Public/media**: is there a public dimension? Who owns the narrative?

**4. Resource Mobilization**
What resources do you need to move and how fast can you move them:
- **Budget**: does the response require unplanned spending? Who authorizes it?
- **People**: do you need to reassign people? From where? What does that break?
- **Technology**: does the response require technical changes? What's the lead time?
- **External support**: do you need outside help (legal, PR, consultants)? Are relationships already in place?

**5. Decision Points**
Identify the moments where the plan might break or fork:
- **Go/no-go decisions**: where does someone have to make a call with incomplete information?
- **Escalation triggers**: what would make this worse? At what point does the response plan need to escalate to a higher level?
- **Pivot points**: where might you need to abandon the current response and switch to a different approach?
- **Time pressure**: which decisions have real deadlines vs. which feel urgent but can wait?

**6. Contingency Options**
For each decision point, define the backup:
- **If the primary response fails**: what's plan B?
- **If the situation escalates**: what's the next level of response?
- **If information changes**: what new information would change the response entirely?
- **Worst case**: if everything goes wrong simultaneously, what's the minimum viable response that preserves the most value?

Deliver as a tabletop exercise report. Mark every point where the plan is untested, under-resourced, or depends on assumptions. The purpose is to find the cracks before the event does.

## Tips

- The most common finding is "we don't know who owns the initial response." If the first 30 minutes are spent figuring out who's in charge, you've already lost time you can't get back.
- Test the plan against the trigger as described, not a softer version. If the trigger is "our largest customer announces they're switching to Competitor B," don't water it down to "a customer is evaluating alternatives."
- Pay attention to cascading failures. The response to one event often creates new problems. A price cut to retain customers might trigger a margin crisis that triggers a board conversation.
- The best tabletop exercises end with a short list of things to fix now, not a long list of things to worry about later.
