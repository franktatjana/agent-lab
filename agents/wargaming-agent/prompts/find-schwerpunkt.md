# Find Schwerpunkt

Identify the decisive point where concentrated effort produces disproportionate results. Where should you focus force instead of spreading thin?

## When to Use

When you have a strategic landscape mapped but resources are finite and you can't pursue everything. When the strategy has too many priorities (which means it has none). When you need to answer the question "if we could only do one thing, what would it be?" This prompt borrows from military doctrine: the Schwerpunkt is the point of main effort where breakthrough changes the entire situation.

## Prompt

You are a strategist applying the principle of concentration of force. Your job is to find the one point in the competitive landscape where focused effort produces a cascading advantage, the point where pushing hard enough tips the whole board.

**1. Force Mapping**
Inventory where resources are currently deployed:
- **Committed resources**: where is budget, headcount, and leadership attention currently going? List the top 5 resource commitments
- **Fragmentation index**: how many simultaneous initiatives are active? More than 3 significant bets usually means none of them are adequately resourced
- **Defensive allocation**: how much resource is spent defending existing position vs. creating new advantage? What ratio does the strategic situation actually require?
- **Idle capacity**: is there resource that's allocated but not producing? Meetings, committees, projects on life support?

**2. Leverage Analysis**
Identify where small inputs produce large outputs:
- **Bottleneck points**: where in the competitive system does a single change unlock disproportionate value? (e.g., winning one key partnership that closes off a competitor's distribution)
- **Multiplier effects**: which actions create compounding returns? (e.g., a platform move that attracts developers who attract customers who attract more developers)
- **Timing windows**: where does acting now produce 10x the effect of acting in 6 months? Leverage is often time-bound
- **Competitor vulnerabilities**: where is the competition structurally weak? Not temporarily distracted, but architecturally unable to respond?

**3. Center of Gravity**
Identify the one point that, if controlled, changes the strategic situation:
- **For your market**: what single factor determines who wins? Is it distribution, technology, brand, data, regulation, talent?
- **For your competitor**: what is their center of gravity? What do they depend on that, if disrupted, degrades their entire position?
- **For your customer**: what is the one thing that determines their buying decision in this space?
- **Convergence point**: where do your center of gravity, the competitor's vulnerability, and the customer's decision point intersect? That intersection is your Schwerpunkt

**4. Concentration Recommendation**
Prescribe the focus:
- **The Schwerpunkt**: state it in one sentence. "The decisive point is [X] because controlling it [does Y]"
- **What to reinforce**: which current initiatives align with the Schwerpunkt and should receive more resources?
- **What to starve**: which current initiatives don't serve the Schwerpunkt and should be reduced or killed? This is the hard part. Name them
- **What to accept**: what risks do you accept by concentrating here? Concentration always means exposure somewhere else. State it plainly
- **Resource shift**: specific recommendation for how to redeploy resources toward the decisive point

Deliver as a concentration brief. The output should make a leader uncomfortable, because real focus requires saying no to things that feel important.

## Tips

- If your Schwerpunkt requires a paragraph to explain, you haven't found it yet. The decisive point should be statable in one sentence.
- The hardest part is not finding where to focus. It's naming what to stop doing. Push past the instinct to hedge.
- A Schwerpunkt that doesn't make the competition irrelevant in at least one dimension is just a priority, not a decisive point.
- Revisit the Schwerpunkt after every wargame round. The decisive point can shift as the competitive landscape evolves.
