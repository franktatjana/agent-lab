# Debrief

After-action review: what was expected to happen, what actually happened in the simulation, why the gap exists, and what strategy adjustments are needed.

## When to Use

After completing one or more wargame rounds, or after a real competitive event that you want to learn from. The debrief is where simulation becomes strategy. Without it, you ran an interesting exercise and learned nothing. This prompt applies formal after-action review methodology to wargame outputs.

## Prompt

You are conducting an after-action review of a wargame simulation (or a real competitive event). Your job is to extract the lessons that change future behavior, not just document what happened.

**1. Expected Outcome**
Before the simulation ran, what did you expect to happen?
- What was your hypothesis going in?
- What did you expect competitors to do?
- What market reaction did you predict?
- What confidence level did you assign to these predictions?

Be honest about priors. The value of the debrief is in the gap between expectation and result. If you retroactively adjust your expectations to match what happened, you've wasted the exercise.

**2. Actual Outcome**
What did the simulation (or real event) produce?
- What moves were made by each player?
- How did the market respond?
- What was the end-state after all moves resolved?
- What surprised you?

**3. Gap Analysis**
For every significant difference between expected and actual:
- **What happened differently?** State the specific divergence
- **Why?** Trace the divergence back to its cause. Was it a competitor behaving unexpectedly? A market dynamic you missed? A timing issue?
- **Severity**: is this gap a minor adjustment or a fundamental challenge to the strategy?
- **Category**: classify each gap as information gap (you didn't know something), analysis gap (you had the data but drew the wrong conclusion), or assumption gap (your mental model was wrong)

**4. Assumption Audit**
Every strategy rests on assumptions. List the assumptions that held and the ones that broke:
- **Validated assumptions**: which beliefs were confirmed by the simulation? These are now higher-confidence inputs for future planning
- **Broken assumptions**: which beliefs were contradicted? These are the most valuable findings. For each broken assumption, state what replaces it
- **Untested assumptions**: which beliefs were never challenged because the simulation didn't create the conditions? These are still risks

**5. Strategy Adjustments**
Based on the gap analysis and assumption audit:
- **Immediate changes**: what should change in the strategy right now?
- **Contingency additions**: what new scenarios should you prepare for that you weren't considering before?
- **Monitoring triggers**: what signals should you watch for that would indicate the competitive landscape is shifting in a direction the wargame revealed?
- **Kill criteria**: is there anything the simulation revealed that should make you abandon the current strategy entirely?

**6. Key Lessons**
Distill to 3-5 lessons. Each lesson should be:
- Specific enough to act on
- Connected to evidence from the simulation
- Applicable beyond this single scenario

Format: "[What we learned] because [what happened in the simulation], which means [what we should do differently]."

Deliver as an after-action report. The purpose is not to feel good about the simulation. The purpose is to update beliefs and change behavior.

## Tips

- The most valuable debrief findings are broken assumptions, not competitor moves. Competitor moves change quarterly. Bad mental models persist for years.
- If the debrief produces no strategy changes, either the simulation confirmed your strategy (good) or you're not being honest about the gaps (bad). Know which one.
- Run the debrief immediately after the simulation while the reasoning is fresh. "We'll debrief next week" means "we'll never debrief."
- Include dissenting views. If someone in the room saw the gap coming and was overruled, that's a process lesson as important as any strategic one.
