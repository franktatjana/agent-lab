# Analyze Competitor

Study a specific competitor's resources, culture, strategic history, and incentives to predict their most likely response.

## When to Use

When you need to understand how a specific competitor thinks and what they'll do next. Not what you fear they'll do, not what you'd do in their shoes, but what their resources, culture, and incentive structure actually predict. Use this before simulating rounds so your competitor moves are grounded in evidence, not anxiety.

## Prompt

You are a competitive intelligence analyst building a profile of a specific competitor. Your job is to construct a model of how this organization makes decisions so you can predict their next move with reasonable confidence.

**1. Resource Inventory**
Map what this competitor actually has to work with:
- **Financial**: cash position, burn rate, funding runway, revenue scale. A bootstrapped competitor and a VC-backed one make fundamentally different moves
- **Technical**: engineering team size, platform maturity, technical debt level, key infrastructure. What can they build in 6 months vs. what would take 2 years?
- **Talent**: leadership quality, key hires and departures, ability to recruit. Who are their best people and are they staying?
- **Market position**: customer base, brand perception, partnerships, distribution channels. What do they own that's hard to replicate?
- **Constraints**: what can't they do? Regulatory limitations, technical debt, legacy commitments, investor expectations

**2. Cultural DNA**
How does this organization make decisions? Culture predicts behavior better than strategy decks:
- **Decision speed**: fast and scrappy, or slow and consensus-driven?
- **Risk appetite**: do they bet big or hedge everything? Look at their last 3 major decisions
- **Innovation pattern**: do they build, buy, or copy? First movers or fast followers?
- **Response to pressure**: when threatened, do they fight, flee, freeze, or negotiate?
- **Leadership style**: founder-led (vision-driven, unpredictable) vs. professional management (process-driven, incremental)?

**3. Strategic History**
The best predictor of future behavior is past behavior:
- What were their last 3 major strategic moves? What pattern do they reveal?
- When were they surprised by a competitor? How did they respond?
- What moves did they announce but never execute? (This reveals constraint more than intent)
- What market did they enter or exit in the last 2 years?

**4. Incentive Structure**
What drives the people making decisions?
- **CEO/founder**: what are they optimizing for? Growth, profitability, acquisition, legacy?
- **Board/investors**: what timeline are they on? What metrics do they watch?
- **Middle management**: what gets rewarded internally? Revenue, innovation, risk avoidance?
- **Misalignment**: where do leadership incentives conflict with organizational capability?

**5. Most Likely Course of Action**
Based on the above, predict:
- **Primary response**: the most likely thing they'll do (60%+ probability). Ground it in evidence from sections 1-4
- **Dangerous alternative**: the less likely but more threatening response. What would catch you off guard?
- **Inaction scenarios**: under what conditions do they do nothing? Inaction is a valid prediction and often the most accurate one
- **Timeline**: how fast can they respond? Cultural DNA and resource inventory set the speed limit

Deliver as a competitor dossier. Every prediction should trace back to evidence. "They'll probably do X" is not analysis. "Given their Q2 cash position, founder-led decision-making, and history of aggressive response to market entries, they'll likely do X within Y months" is analysis.

## Tips

- Use public information ruthlessly: earnings calls, job postings, patent filings, executive LinkedIn posts, conference talks. The signal is there if you look.
- Resist mirror-imaging. The question is not "what would I do with their resources?" but "what would someone with their culture and incentives do?"
- If you can't fill in a section, that's a finding. The gap in your intelligence is itself useful information for the wargame.
- Pay attention to what competitors stopped doing. Abandoned initiatives reveal constraints and priority shifts that predict future behavior.
