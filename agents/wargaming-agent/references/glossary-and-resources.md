# Glossary and Resources

Core terminology and external resources for the Wargaming Agent's competitive simulation methodology.

## When to Load

Always available as baseline reference. Load when defining terms, grounding analysis in established doctrine, or providing framework context to users new to wargaming methodology.

## Key Terms

**Schwerpunkt**: German for "hard point" or "center of gravity." The decisive point where concentrated effort produces disproportionate results. Choosing the Schwerpunkt means accepting risk everywhere else.

**OODA Loop**: Observe-Orient-Decide-Act. John Boyd's decision cycle. The side that cycles faster gains initiative. The Orient phase, where mental models interpret observations, is where most strategic failures occur.

**Red Team**: The adversarial force in a simulation. Thinks and acts as the competitor, using the competitor's actual capabilities and incentives. The job is to destroy the strategy before a real opponent does.

**Blue Team**: The defending force in a simulation. Represents your organization's strategy as it responds to Red Team attacks. Tests whether the strategy can adapt under pressure.

**Kriegsspiel**: The original Prussian military wargame (1824). Opposing teams, shared map, umpire adjudication. Introduced friction and uncertainty into strategic planning.

**After-Action Review (AAR)**: Structured debrief after a simulation. Compares what was planned to what happened, extracts lessons, and produces concrete strategy adjustments. The debrief is where the real value lives.

**Tabletop Exercise (TTX)**: Scenario-based walkthrough testing response plans without live execution. Exposes gaps in decision authority, communication, and contingency planning.

**Center of Gravity**: The source of power that provides an organization its freedom of action, strength, or will to act. Clausewitz's concept, operationalized through Schwerpunkt.

**Tempo**: The rate of activity relative to the opponent. Controlling tempo means forcing the opponent to react on your schedule rather than theirs.

**Friction**: Clausewitz's concept that everything in strategy is simple, but the simplest thing is difficult. Plans degrade on contact with reality through delays, miscommunication, resource shortages, and human error.

**Fog of War**: Uncertainty in operations. Incomplete intelligence, ambiguous signals, and the gap between what you think the opponent is doing and what they are actually doing.

**Commander's Intent**: A clear statement of the desired end state that allows subordinates to adapt when conditions change without waiting for new orders. Enables decentralized execution under uncertainty.

## Recommended Reading

**Business War Games** by Benjamin Gilad. The definitive guide to applying military wargaming methodology to corporate strategy. Practical frameworks for multi-round competitive simulations.

**Wargaming for Leaders** by Mark Herman, Mark Frost, and Robert Kurz. Bridges military wargaming and executive decision-making with structured approaches for testing strategy.

**Red Team** by Micah Zenko. How organizations use adversarial thinking to expose blind spots and test assumptions. Essential reading for understanding why friendly rooms produce dangerous strategies.

**The Art of War** by Sun Tzu. The foundational text on competitive strategy, terrain assessment, and the psychology of conflict. Read for principles, not prescriptions.

**Certain to Win** by Chet Richards. Applies Boyd's OODA Loop and maneuver warfare concepts to business competition. The most practical bridge between Boyd's military thinking and corporate strategy.

## Related Agents

**Corporate Navigator Agent**: Receives handoffs when wargame simulations reveal stakeholder dynamics, political landscapes, and organizational power structures that need deeper mapping beyond the competitive scenario.

**Why Agent**: Receives handoffs when after-action reviews surface root causes that need structured investigation using the Five Whys or causal analysis methodology.

**Six Hats Agent**: Feeds into the Wargaming Agent when a strategic question needs structured multi-perspective analysis (facts, emotions, risks, benefits, creativity, process) before entering a competitive simulation.
