# Wargaming Frameworks

Military and business wargaming draws from a tradition stretching back centuries, from Prussian officer training rooms to modern corporate strategy labs. These six frameworks represent the core methodologies the Wargaming Agent uses to structure competitive simulations. Each framework addresses a different aspect of strategic testing: some focus on multi-party simulation, others on adversarial stress-testing, and others on decision speed and resource concentration.

## When to Load

Load when explaining methodology to users, when selecting the right framework for a specific strategic question, or when the simulation needs deeper grounding in established doctrine.

## Kriegsspiel (Prussian Wargame)

The original military wargame, developed by Georg von Reisswitz and adopted by the Prussian army in 1824. Two opposing teams make moves on a shared map representing real terrain, with an umpire adjudicating outcomes based on probability tables and military doctrine. The critical innovation was friction: plans that looked brilliant on paper met supply problems, communication delays, and the fog of war on the game board. Prussian officers who trained with Kriegsspiel developed an instinct for planning under uncertainty that gave them a decisive edge in the Franco-Prussian War.

**Maps to:** Business strategy testing where real resources are at stake. Multiple teams simulate their company, competitors, and market forces on a shared competitive landscape. An adjudicator introduces market friction, regulatory changes, and customer behavior shifts that no team predicted. The value is in watching plans collide with adversarial responses, not in the plan itself.

**Source:** Georg von Reisswitz, "Instructions for the Representation of Tactical Maneuvers under the Guise of a Wargame" (1824). Public domain military heritage.

## Business Wargaming (Gilad)

Benjamin Gilad's methodology adapted military wargaming for corporate strategy. Teams role-play as competitors, customers, regulators, and their own company across multiple rounds. Each round represents a time period (quarter, year), and each team makes strategic moves based on their assigned organization's real capabilities, culture, and incentives. The power of Gilad's approach is forcing strategists to think from inside a competitor's decision-making process, not from their own perspective looking out.

**Maps to:** Pre-launch strategy testing, competitive response planning, market entry analysis. Particularly effective when a company is considering a major strategic move (acquisition, new product line, market entry) and needs to model how the competitive landscape reshapes itself in response.

**Source:** Benjamin Gilad, "Business War Games" (2008). Published methodology.

## Red Team / Blue Team

Adversarial simulation where a Red Team attacks the strategy and a Blue Team defends it. The Red Team's job is to destroy the plan using the tools, resources, and thinking that a real adversary would employ. The Blue Team adapts and defends. The methodology originated in military intelligence and has been widely adopted in cybersecurity, but its application to business strategy is equally powerful. The core principle is that assumptions which survive only in friendly rooms are the most dangerous kind.

**Maps to:** Security testing, strategy stress-testing, proposal defense, M&A due diligence. The Red Team should be staffed with people who genuinely understand the competitive landscape, not assigned as a token exercise. The best Red Team findings are the ones that make the Blue Team uncomfortable.

**Source:** Micah Zenko, "Red Team: How to Succeed by Thinking Like the Enemy" (2015). Published methodology.

## Tabletop Exercise (TTX)

A scenario-based walkthrough where participants respond to an unfolding situation: "If this happens, what do we do?" The scenario is presented in phases, each revealing new information and forcing revised decisions. No live execution occurs; the value is in exposing gaps in response plans, decision authority, and communication chains before a real crisis tests them. Tabletop exercises originated in emergency management and military contingency planning.

**Maps to:** Crisis planning, regulatory response, competitive contingency planning, supply chain disruption scenarios. Effective for testing whether the organization knows who decides what when speed matters. The most revealing moment in a TTX is when participants discover they assumed someone else was responsible for a critical decision.

**Source:** Standard methodology in emergency management and military doctrine. FEMA Homeland Security Exercise and Evaluation Program (HSEEP) provides public frameworks.

## OODA Loop (Boyd)

John Boyd's Observe-Orient-Decide-Act cycle, developed from his experience as a fighter pilot and his study of military history. Boyd's core insight was that the side that cycles through OODA faster gains the initiative, while the slower side is perpetually reacting to a reality that has already changed. The critical phase is Orient, the mental model through which observations are interpreted. Most strategic failures happen in Orient, when decision-makers see new data through old frameworks and misinterpret what is happening.

**Maps to:** Competitive tempo, market response speed, decision agility. The OODA Loop is not about being fast for speed's sake; it is about being faster than the opponent's ability to adapt. In business, this means recognizing market shifts, reorienting strategy, and executing changes before competitors have finished their committee meetings.

**Source:** John Boyd, "Patterns of Conflict" (1986). Public domain military doctrine.

## Schwerpunkt (Center of Gravity)

German military concept meaning "hard point" or "center of gravity." The principle is to identify the critical point where concentrated effort produces disproportionate results, then focus all available force on that point. Spreading resources evenly across a broad front is the strategy of an organization that does not know what matters most. Schwerpunkt demands clarity about where the decisive battle will be fought and the discipline to accept risk everywhere else.

**Maps to:** Resource allocation, strategic focus, decisive action. In business terms, Schwerpunkt answers the question: "If we could only win in one area, which area would cause everything else to fall into place?" It is the antidote to the strategy of trying to be competitive everywhere simultaneously, which is the strategy of being decisive nowhere.

**Source:** German military doctrine, extensively analyzed in Robert Citino, "The German Way of War" (2005). Carl von Clausewitz, "On War" (1832), for the original center of gravity concept. Public domain.
