# Agent Handbook

A narrative guide to every agent in the Agent Lab. Each entry explains the problem the agent solves, the trap most people fall into, and how the agent changes the outcome. Use this handbook to understand when and why to use each agent, not just what it does.

For technical specifications, see the [agent definitions](../agents/). For design methodology, see the [design handbook](handbook.md).

---

## How to Use This Guide

Every agent guide follows the same structure:

- **The Problem**: the real-world situation where this agent is valuable
- **The Trap**: the obvious approach that usually fails
- **How It Helps**: the perspective shift or framework that changes the outcome
- **A Story**: a narrative scenario showing the agent in action
- **Step-by-Step**: how to engage the agent
- **Skills, Personalities, Escalation**: practical reference for choosing the right configuration

Start with the decision tree below to find the right agent for your situation, then read the individual guide for depth.

## Agent Directory

### Perspective & Reframing

Agents that help you see the same situation through a different lens.

#### [Cat POV Agent](guides/cat-pov-agent-guide.md)

Observes workplace behavior through feline behavioral science: territorial intelligence, selective attention, energy conservation. When you cannot see your own patterns, a non-human lens makes the invisible visible.

**Best for**: boundary problems masquerading as workload problems, energy management, meeting overload.
**Frameworks**: Territorial Intelligence, Selective Attention, Energy Conservation.

#### [Superhero Agent](guides/superhero-agent-guide.md)

Reframes challenges through Marvel archetypes. Matches your strengths to hero types, names your obstacles as villains, and builds confidence through narrative framing.

**Best for**: imposter syndrome, role transitions, confidence before high-stakes moments.
**Archetypes**: Iron Man, Captain America, Black Widow, Spider-Man, Thor, Hulk, Black Panther, Doctor Strange.

---

### Communication & Translation

Agents that help messages land with intended meaning across different audiences.

#### [Culture Agent](guides/culture-agent-guide.md)

Translates communication between cultural backgrounds using dimensional analysis. Explains why the same message lands differently across cultures and provides adapted alternatives.

**Best for**: cross-cultural meetings, international team friction, message adaptation.
**Frameworks**: Hofstede Dimensions, Meyer Culture Map, Hall High/Low Context.

#### [Generation Agent](guides/generation-agent-guide.md)

Bridges generational communication styles. Reframes differences as translation problems, not character flaws. Adapts messages while preserving intent.

**Best for**: cross-generational friction, communication style mismatches, team norms negotiation.
**Generations**: Boomer, Gen X, Millennial, Gen Z, Gen Alpha.

#### [Question Decoder Agent](guides/question-decoder-agent-guide.md)

Decodes questions before answering them. Analyzes who is asking, why they are asking, and what they need to hear. Architects responses at the right altitude for the audience.

**Best for**: board questions, stakeholder Q&A, answering at the wrong level.
**Frameworks**: Three-Lens Decoding (Who/Why/What).

#### [Storytelling Agent](guides/storytelling-agent-guide.md)

Transforms facts, data, and experiences into stories that move people to action. Selects narrative framework, builds tension and resolution, adapts for audience and medium.

**Best for**: presenting bad news, quarterly reviews, pitches, origin stories, data communication.
**Frameworks**: Hero's Journey, Pixar Story Spine, STAR, Data Storytelling.

---

### Interpersonal & Leadership

Agents that help navigate people dynamics, conversations, and team development.

#### [Difficult Conversations Agent](guides/difficult-conversations-agent-guide.md)

Prepares for challenging conversations by separating three layers: What Happened, Feelings, and Identity. Maps both perspectives, helps you own your contribution, and prepares for reactions.

**Best for**: delivering hard feedback, addressing behavior, relationship repair after conflict.
**Frameworks**: Three Conversations Model, Nonviolent Communication, Crucial Conversations.

#### [Leadership Coach Agent](guides/leadership-coach-agent-guide.md)

Coaches leaders through people challenges. Assesses style, diagnoses team dynamics, and builds development plans. Asks before advising.

**Best for**: new leaders, psychological safety issues, team health, leadership style adaptation.
**Frameworks**: GROW Model, Situational Leadership, Psychological Safety, Radical Candor.

#### [Networking Agent](guides/networking-agent-guide.md)

Helps build and maintain professional relationships strategically. Treats networking as a learnable skill built on genuine value exchange, not personality.

**Best for**: career visibility, network architecture, relationship maintenance, warm introductions.
**Frameworks**: Weak Ties Theory, Give First, Dunbar's Numbers.

#### [Corporate Navigator Agent](guides/corporate-navigator-agent-guide.md)

Maps invisible workplace power structures. Names political dynamics explicitly (gatekeeping, coalition, sponsorship) and develops ethical strategies for navigating them.

**Best for**: blocked proposals, unclear decision-making, organizational politics, career strategy.
**Frameworks**: Stakeholder Mapping, Power/Interest Grid, Influence Planning.

---

### Analysis & Decision-Making

Agents that help structure thinking, investigate causes, and test strategies.

#### [Six Hats Agent](guides/six-hats-agent-guide.md)

Forces parallel thinking: everyone examines the same perspective at the same time. Prevents the common failure modes of mixed-mode debate.

**Best for**: team decisions, idea evaluation, risk assessment, breaking debate deadlocks.
**Frameworks**: de Bono's Six Thinking Hats.

#### [Why Agent](guides/why-agent-guide.md)

Drills from symptoms to root causes through structured questioning. Validates at each level: is this a root cause or still a symptom?

**Best for**: recurring problems, disengaged team members, process failures, systemic issues.
**Frameworks**: 5 Whys, Fishbone Analysis, A3 Problem Solving.

#### [Design Thinking Agent](guides/design-thinking-agent-guide.md)

Guides human-centered problem-solving from empathy through testing. Forces understanding of users before proposing solutions.

**Best for**: product problems, service redesign, finding the real problem before building.
**Frameworks**: Stanford d.school 5-Stage, Empathy Mapping, HMW Questions.

#### [Research Agent](guides/research-agent-guide.md)

Finds and synthesizes information from multiple sources with citations. Treats research as scoped hypothesis-testing, not exhaustive cataloging. Names gaps explicitly.

**Best for**: market analysis, fact-checking, topic orientation, competitive intelligence.
**Output**: summary, cited key points, source credibility, confidence level, gaps.

#### [Wargaming Agent](guides/wargaming-agent-guide.md)

Runs competitive simulations against thinking opponents. Before committing resources, plays out the strategy to see how competitors, regulators, and markets respond.

**Best for**: market entry, competitive strategy, resource allocation, strategic pivots.
**Frameworks**: Kriegsspiel, OODA Loop, Red Team/Blue Team, Schwerpunkt.

## Cross-Agent Patterns

Several patterns appear across all 15 agents that explain why they work:

**Perspective shift is the primary value.** Agents help users see the same situation through a different lens: cat eyes, cultural dimensions, generational translation, villain archetypes, thinking hats. The information does not change. The frame does.

**The real problem is rarely what it appears to be.** The blocked proposal is not about regulatory risk, it is about resource burden. The bad interface is not a usability problem, it is a motivation problem. The disengaged performer is not burned out, he does not understand advancement criteria. Every agent is designed to look past the surface.

**Framework first, then content.** Users provide messy situations. Agents structure them through decision, analysis, or narrative frameworks that make invisible patterns visible. The framework is the lens.

**Timing matters.** Most agents are most valuable before decisions are finalized, before high-stakes conversations happen, before resources are committed. After-the-fact analysis is useful but less impactful than pre-engagement preparation.

**Personality selection shapes the conversation type.** Business personality for boardroom contexts. Coach for thinking partnerships. Educator for knowledge transfer. Strategic for high-stakes moves. Choosing the right personality is as important as choosing the right agent.
